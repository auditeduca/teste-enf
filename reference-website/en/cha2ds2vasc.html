About this tool

To estimate the annual risk of stroke in patients with non-valvular atrial fibrillation, guiding the indication for anticoagulant therapy. Indicated for all patients diagnosed with non-valvular atrial fibrillation as part of thromboembolic risk assessment.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–0: Very Low Risk
  • 1–1: Low Risk
  • 2–9: Moderate to High Risk

Important tips

  • The point assigned for female sex alone (without other risk factors) generally does not justify anticoagulation alone.
  • Always assess CHA2DS2-VASc together with bleeding risk (HAS-BLED) for a balanced therapeutic decision.
  • Reassess the score periodically, as risk factors such as age and comorbidities may change over time.
  • The final decision on anticoagulation should involve shared discussion with the patient about risks and benefits.

    Foundation

    The CHA2DS2-VASc score was developed as a refinement of the CHADS2 score, including additional risk factors (vascular disease, age 65-74 years, and female sex), to more accurately estimate the annual stroke risk in patients with non-valvular atrial fibrillation.

    History

    Widely recommended by major international cardiology guidelines (including the European Society of Cardiology) as the standard tool for decision-making regarding anticoagulation in atrial fibrillation.

    NANDA — Risk for Ineffective Cerebral Tissue Perfusion

    At risk for decrease in cerebral blood circulation that may compromise health.

    NANDA — Risk for bleeding

    Susceptibility to a decrease in blood volume that may compromise health, associated with anticoagulant therapy.

    NANDA — Deficient Knowledge

    Absence or deficiency of cognitive information related to a specific topic, such as prescribed anticoagulant therapy.

    NOC — Risk control: thromboembolism

    • Adherence to anticoagulant therapeutic regimen
    • Recognition of signs of bleeding
    • Attendance at follow-up appointments

    NIC — Circulatory care: arterial insufficiency

    • Calculate the CHA2DS2-VASc score at initial assessment and periodically
    • Educate about the importance of adherence to prescribed anticoagulation
    • Monitor for signs of bleeding and thromboembolic events
    • Jointly assess bleeding risk (HAS-BLED)

    Scientific Validation

    Extensively validated in cardiology literature, with good predictive ability for thromboembolic risk, being the tool of choice in most current atrial fibrillation management guidelines.

    Limitations

    It should always be assessed together with bleeding risk (e.g., HAS-BLED score) for a balanced therapeutic decision on anticoagulation. The point assigned solely for female sex should not, by itself, justify anticoagulation.

    How CHA2DS2-VASc works

    CHA2DS2-VASc sums points from 7 risk factors — congestive heart failure, hypertension, age, diabetes, prior stroke/TIA, vascular disease, and female sex — with different weights (1 or 2 points), totaling 0 to 9 points, which estimate the annual stroke risk in atrial fibrillation.

    Quick guide: 0 = Very low risk | 1 = Low risk | ≥2 = Moderate to high risk, anticoagulation generally recommended.

    Should CHA2DS2-VASc be used in valvular atrial fibrillation?

    No. CHA2DS2-VASc was validated for non-valvular atrial fibrillation. Patients with significant valvular disease (e.g., rheumatic mitral stenosis, mechanical prosthetic valve) have indication for anticoagulation based on their own criteria, regardless of the score.

    Does a CHA2DS2-VASc score of 1 always indicate anticoagulation?

    Depends on the context: if the only point is exclusively for female sex, anticoagulation alone is generally not recommended. In other cases of score 1, the decision should be individualized, also assessing bleeding risk.
