About this tool

Estimate the probability of needing hospital intervention (transfusion, therapeutic endoscopy, or surgery) in patients with upper gastrointestinal bleeding, helping to identify low-risk patients eligible for outpatient management. Indicated for patients with suspected or confirmed upper GI bleeding (hematemesis, melena) in the emergency department.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–0: Very Low Risk
  • 1–11: Risk Present
  • 12–23: High Risk

Important tips

  • A Glasgow-Blatchford score of 0 identifies very low-risk patients who may be considered for outpatient management in appropriate clinical and social contexts.
  • Unlike other scores, the Glasgow-Blatchford does not require endoscopy results to be calculated, making it useful for initial triage.
  • Hemoglobin has different cutoffs for men and women — pay attention when selecting the correct option.
  • Continuously reassess the patient's vital signs, as gastrointestinal bleeding can evolve rapidly.

    Foundation

    The Glasgow-Blatchford Score was developed by Oliver Blatchford and colleagues, combining simple clinical and laboratory data (urea, hemoglobin, systolic blood pressure, heart rate, melena, syncope, hepatic disease, and heart failure) to predict the need for hospital intervention in patients with upper gastrointestinal bleeding, without requiring prior endoscopy.

    History

    Widely used in emergency departments to identify low-risk patients (score 0) who could be safely considered for outpatient management, optimizing hospital resources and reducing unnecessary admissions.

    NANDA — Risk for deficient fluid volume

    Risk of decreased intravascular, interstitial, and/or intracellular fluid volume related to active blood loss.

    NANDA — Ineffective peripheral tissue perfusion

    Decrease in blood circulation to the periphery, which may compromise health.

    NANDA — Risk for shock

    Risk of inadequate blood flow to body tissues, which may lead to potentially fatal cellular dysfunction.

    NOC — Severity of blood loss

    • Stable vital signs
    • Absence of signs of hypovolemic shock
    • Stable or recovering hemoglobin

    NIC — Hemorrhage control

    • Apply the Glasgow-Blatchford Score on initial assessment
    • Monitor vital signs continuously
    • Assist in obtaining large-bore venous access and fluid resuscitation
    • Collect laboratory tests as ordered (hemoglobin, urea)
    • Prepare patient for upper endoscopy as indicated

    Scientific Validation

    Extensively validated in the literature, with excellent sensitivity for identifying patients who will require intervention, considered by many guidelines to be superior to other scores (such as the pre-endoscopic Rockall) for this specific purpose.

    Limitations

    It does not directly assess the cause of bleeding, being complementary, not a substitute, for upper endoscopy for definitive diagnosis and treatment. The urea values used in this calculator consider approximate conversion from the original cutoffs in mmol/L.

    How does the Glasgow-Blatchford Score work?

    The Glasgow-Blatchford Score sums weighted points from 8 clinical and laboratory variables — urea, hemoglobin, systolic blood pressure, heart rate, melena, syncope, hepatic disease, and cardiac failure — totaling 0 to 23 points.

    Quick guide: 0 = Very low risk (consider outpatient management) | 1-11 = Risk present | ≥12 = High risk, urgent hospital intervention.

    Does the Glasgow-Blatchford replace upper endoscopy?

    No. It is an initial risk triage tool calculated before endoscopy. Endoscopy remains essential for diagnosing the cause of bleeding and definitive treatment when indicated.

    In which unit are the urea values in this calculator?

    The values have been converted to mg/dL (the unit most commonly used in Brazilian laboratories) from the original cutoffs in mmol/L published in the original study.
