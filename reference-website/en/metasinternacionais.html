The 6 International Patient Safety Goals (WHO/JCI) | Complete Guide

International Patient Safety Goals

The international patient safety goals are guidelines established to ensure a safe environment and reduce risks in healthcare. They are a joint effort of the World Health Organization (WHO) and the Joint Commission International (JCI).

Implementing these goals contributes to a safer care environment, reducing patient harm, promoting quality of care, and optimizing health outcomes. When using our scales and calculators, you will often see a reference to a goal according to the scale or calculator; this aims to reinforce attention to care and work on health education.

The 6 International Patient Safety Goals are:

  • Identify patients correctly
    Ensure that the patient receives the correct care and procedures, using at least two identifiers (e.g., full name and date of birth).

    Measures:

    • Identification wristband with full name, date of birth, mother's name, ward and bed, medical record number.
    • Bedside identification plate
    • Verify that the patient's complete data are exactly the same in the medical record, medical prescription, and laboratory tests.
    • Verbally verify with the patient and companion the full name, date of birth, mother's name, and presence of allergies.
    • Check in the system if the patient's FAA is the same as the medical record files.
    • Do not leave two patients with the same name in the same room; if this occurs, notify your nurse, the nurse notifies the Internal Bed Regulation Center (NIR), and request the transfer of one of the patients to another room.
    • Identification wristbands for allergies, fall risk, and aspiration risk.
    • Never say the patient's full name for the patient to confirm; this may lead the patient to error. Ask questions without expecting the correct answer from the patient regarding their data.
  • Improve effective communication
    Ensure that important patient information is communicated clearly, completely, and timely, especially during care transitions.

    Measures (examples):

    • In the same unit, perform bedside handover during shift change with SBAR and medical prescriptions in hand.
    • Patient admission: (From origin) to destination (Your ward), whether in person or by phone contact, the important thing is that everything is passed on: diagnostic hypothesis, age, brief hospitalization history, established conduct so far, allergies, previous surgeries, medications the patient is using, comorbidities, any type of isolation, presence of lesions, tests, presence or absence of companion, etc.
    • Admission and the (NIR): The internal regulation center should contact you informing about the admission; if you already have data on isolation, blood culture, companion, etc., pass this information to the NIR; often the bed regulated for this patient is not the most appropriate due to these criteria.
    • Patients going to the operating room: If you are the operating room nurse, perform the preoperative nursing visit. If you are referring a patient to the operating room, the handoff must be reported describing the treatment plan established so far, history, comorbidities, allergies, and all items of the surgical checklist in person or by phone. It is important that all essential information is reported and all forms are checked and stamped. Whoever refers the patient to the operating room performs all steps of the handoff and surgical checklist again.
    • Nurse-physician communication: If your matter is properly about a patient, try to know your patient. There are many patients, sometimes even the physician does not know their patients by full name and bed. Know the patient of the clinical discussion, check recent exams, try to be up to date with the treatment and clinical situation of the patient at the moment you request medical support.
    • Double and triple checking of prescribed medications: Nursing technician, no matter how busy the nurse is, high-alert medications (HAM) require double checking and the presence of the nurse during drug preparation and joint verification of the HAM prescription. Nurse, request clinical support from the clinical pharmacist or the on-call physician to perform triple checking of HAMs.
    • Transfer of patients to other units such as ICU: If the patient has had a clinical deterioration and needs to be transferred to the ICU, nurse, check with the physician about the physician-to-physician contact (ICU). After this contact, perform the handoff to the intensivist nurse: patient identification, age, current respiratory pattern, management established at the time of deterioration, degree of patient complexity at the moment, Glasgow Coma Scale, comorbidities, allergies, isolations, imaging exams and approximate date performed during admission. If the transfer is performed with an advanced airway, the handoff should be done as soon as possible, given that the ICU will need to prepare the ventilator, infusion pumps, etc., to receive the patient.
    • Multidisciplinary meetings and rounds
    • Changes in the patient's clinical status: Nursing technician: report to the nurse changes in vital signs, cognitive behavior, nutritional status, etc. Nurse, in case of (non-severe) changes in the patient's clinical status related to nutrition, mobility, physiological, psychological, and social needs, communicate with the multidisciplinary team.
  • Improve safety in prescribing, using, and administering medications
    Reduce risks related to high-alert medications, prescription errors, storage, and administration of drugs.
  • Ensure correct surgery site, procedure, and patient
    Implement preoperative verification protocols, surgical site marking, and "surgical pause" before starting the procedure.
  • Perform hand hygiene to prevent infections
    Promote hand hygiene practices, antimicrobial stewardship, use of sterile equipment, and environmental control.
  • Reduce the risk of patient harm from falls and pressure injuries
    Assess and monitor patients at risk for falls and pressure injuries and implement preventive measures.

    Measures:

    • Low bed
    • Raised side rails
    • Apply the Morse scale and other fall risk scales
    • Floor always dry
    • Adequate lighting
    • Call bell within patient's reach
    • Fall risk identification
    • Presence of companion, when indicated
    • Perform repositioning in high-complexity patients every 2 hours
    • Use of alternating pressure mattress
    • Use of pillows
    • Application of the Braden Scale and other scales to assess risk for pressure injuries
