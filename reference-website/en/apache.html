About this tool

Calculate the APACHE II score for prognostic and severity assessment of patients admitted to the Intensive Care Unit. Indicated for adult patients in the first 24 hours of ICU admission.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–10: Low risk (mortality < 10%)
  • 11–20: Moderate risk (mortality 10-25%)
  • 21–30: High risk (mortality 25-50%)
  • 31–71: Very high risk (mortality > 50%)

Important tips

  • Use the worst physiological values within the first 24 hours of ICU admission.
  • GCS should be assessed without pharmacological sedation, if possible.
  • Double the creatinine score in cases of acute renal failure.
  • APACHE II does not replace individual clinical judgment.
  • Reassess the score daily to monitor progression.

    Foundation

    The APACHE II (Acute Physiology and Chronic Health Evaluation II) scale was developed by Knaus et al. in 1985 as a severity classification system for adult ICU patients. The score is obtained by summing 12 acute physiological variables (each scored 0 to 4, except GCS which uses 15-GCS), plus points for age (0 to 6) and pre-existing chronic health conditions (0 to 5).

    History

    APACHE I was published in 1981. APACHE II, published in 1985, simplified the original version from 34 to 12 physiological variables. APACHE III (1991) and APACHE IV (2006) introduced adjustments for diagnosis and geographic location, but APACHE II remains the most widely used worldwide due to its simplicity and extensive validation.

    Scientific Validation

    APACHE II has been validated in diverse populations and clinical settings. Studies demonstrate a correlation between the score and hospital mortality. Calibration may vary between institutions and countries, and periodic reassessment of the model in the local population is recommended. The score should be calculated within the first 24 hours of ICU admission.

    Limitations

    APACHE II should not be used for individual decisions regarding limitation of therapy. The score reflects physiological severity but does not consider factors such as quality of care, available resources, or patient preferences. Creatinine score should be doubled in cases of acute renal failure. The scale was developed for adult patients and is not validated for pediatrics.

    How the APACHE II scale works

    The APACHE II (Acute Physiology and Chronic Health Evaluation II) scale assesses the severity of adult ICU patients by summing 12 acute physiological variables, age, and pre-existing chronic health conditions.

    Components:

    • 12 physiological variables (0-4 points each, except GCS: 15-GCS)
    • Age (0-6 points)
    • Chronic conditions (0-5 points)

    Interpretation:

    • 0-10: Low risk (mortality < 10%)
    • 11-20: Moderate risk (mortality 10-25%)
    • 21-30: High risk (mortality 25-50%)
    • 31-71: Very high risk (mortality > 50%)

    The score should be calculated within the first 24 hours of ICU admission, using the worst observed physiological values.

    When should APACHE II be calculated?

    Preferably within the first 24 hours of ICU admission, using the worst physiological values observed during this period.

    Can APACHE II be used for decisions on therapeutic limitation?

    No. APACHE II is a population risk stratification tool and should not be used alone for individual decisions on therapy limitation. Clinical judgment and patient/family preferences are essential.

    Is APACHE II valid for pediatrics?

    No. APACHE II was developed and validated for adults. For pediatric patients, use the PRISM (Pediatric Risk of Mortality) scale.

    What to do if the patient is sedated?

    GCS should be assessed without pharmacological sedation, if possible. If sedation is necessary, document the GCS score before sedation or use the best observed motor response.

    Important Notice

    This tool is for educational and clinical decision support purposes. Results should always be reviewed and validated by a qualified nursing or medical professional, according to patient assessment and institutional protocols. APACHE II does not replace individual clinical judgment.
