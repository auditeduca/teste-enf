About this tool

Assess fall risk in hospitalized adult patients, guiding preventive measures proportional to risk level. Indicated on admission, after any fall, on transfer, and periodically per institutional protocol (usually every 24h or every shift in at-risk patients).

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–24: Low Risk
  • 25–50: Moderate Risk
  • 51–200: High Risk

Important tips

  • Reassess the score every shift in moderate/high-risk patients.
  • Consider the physical environment of the room, not just the numerical score.
  • Involve the patient and family in prevention guidance.
  • Document the interventions performed, not just the score.

    Foundation

    The Morse Fall Scale was developed by Janice Morse in 1989 to predict fall risk in hospitalized patients, assessing 6 factors: history of falls, secondary diagnosis, ambulatory aid, IV therapy, gait, and mental status.

    History

    Widely adopted in patient safety protocols worldwide, including recommendations from ANVISA and the Brazilian Ministry of Health, being one of the most globally used fall risk scales.

    NANDA — Risk for falls

    Increased vulnerability to falls, which can cause physical harm and compromise health.

    NOC — Fall prevention behavior

    • Correct use of assistive devices
    • Requests assistance when needed
    • Risk-free environment

    NIC — Fall prevention

    • Identify cognitive/physical deficits
    • Signal fall risk
    • Educate patient and family
    • Keep bed rails up and bed low

    Scientific Validation

    Validation studies report sensitivity between 78% and 83% and specificity between 40% and 55%, varying by study population.

    Sensitivity: 80% · Specificity: 48%

    Limitations

    Relatively low specificity may classify many patients as moderate/high risk, requiring complementary clinical judgment. Does not replace individualized assessment of environment and clinical condition.

    How the Morse Scale works

    The Morse Fall Scale assesses 6 fall risk factors: history of falling, secondary diagnosis, ambulatory aid, intravenous therapy, gait, and mental status. The sum of points for each item classifies the patient as low, moderate, or high risk for falls.

    Quick guide: 0-24 = Low risk | 25-50 = Moderate risk | ≥51 = High risk.

    How often should I reassess the Morse Scale?

    Reassessment is recommended every shift for moderate/high-risk patients, on admission, after any fall, and with any significant change in clinical status.

    Does the Morse Scale replace clinical assessment of the environment?

    No. The numerical score should be combined with assessment of the physical environment (lighting, floor, furniture) and the clinical judgment of the nursing team.

    Important Notice

    This tool is for educational and clinical decision support purposes. Results should always be reviewed and validated by a qualified nursing professional, according to patient assessment and institutional protocols.
