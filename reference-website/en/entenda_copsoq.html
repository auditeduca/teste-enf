Understand the Copenhagen Psychosocial Questionnaire - COPSOQ

Learn how to apply this questionnaire in the workplace

O Copenhagen Psychosocial Questionnaire (COPSOQ) is a globally recognized scientific instrument, essential for assessing work organization and managing psychosocial risks. It measures workers' perceptions of their routines, cognitive and emotional demands, relationship with management, and possible symptoms of stress and burnout.

The Importance of COPSOQ and the New NR-1

In Brazil, corporate mental health is no longer optional. With the update of Regulatory Standard NR-1coming into effect this month, organizations are now required to map and mitigate threats that affect employees' mental well-being.

In this scenario, COPSOQ emerges as the gold-standard clinical tool adopted by the corporate world and Occupational Health teams (PGR/PCMSO) for strict implementation and compliance with NR-1. Using this questionnaire allows the company to conduct a legal, documented, and reliable assessment of the metrics required by the new text of NR-1.

The 3 Versions of the Questionnaire and Their Differences

The COPSOQ (currently in its second and third clinical iterations) has been structured in a versatile way to adapt to different needs. It has three main versions:

  • Short Version (41 Questions): Intended for quick self-assessments, screenings in small workplaces, and periodic check-ups. It measures the overall stress panorama quickly and objectively.
  • Medium Version (76 Questions): The standard for use by occupational health professionals. It assesses risks in detail, aiding in planning and company audits.
  • Long Version (119 to 148 Questions): The most exhaustive tool, designed primarily for rigorous scientific research, deep leadership mapping, and diagnosis of burnout clinical.

And what changed in the transition from COPSOQ II to COPSOQ III?
The mathematics and backbone remained the same, but COPSOQ III was modernized for the challenges of the current decade. New important psychosocial factors were included, such as the assessment of Cyber Bullying, Illegitimate Tasks, insecurity of working conditions, and organizational engagement metrics.

Step by Step: How to Calculate the COPSOQ Score

To ensure the questionnaire is not just a "collection of opinions," the tool converts emotions into quantitative data. Learn below how to extract results and understand the "traffic light rule" in a very didactic way:

1 The Likert Scale and Base Score

All items in the questionnaire are assessed using a 5-point Likert scale. The scoring of responses strictly follows these values, so each item can be scored from 1 to 5 points. This score follows the direct direction of the selected alternative. For example, responses range from "1 - Never/almost never" to "5 - Always," or from "1 - Not at all/almost nothing" to "5 - Extremely."

Why should it be done this way? The questionnaire does not measure a single generalist concept, but rather dozens of distinct variables, such as health levels, satisfaction degrees, and specific psychosocial risks for each individual. Establishing this direct score from 1 to 5 allows transforming subjective and qualitative perceptions into a standard numerical basis.

2 Mathematical Inversion of Specific Items

Although the vast majority of questions follow the direct scoring we saw in Step 1, there are specific and very important exceptions. Some questions are formulated inversely to the general concept of that group. For the math to work in the end, the scores of these specific items need to be "mirrored" (inverted).

How mathematical inversion works:
• If the person marked 1, the assigned score will be 5.
• If the person marked 2, the assigned score will be 4.
• If the person marked 3, the assigned score remains 3.
• If the person marked 4, the assigned score will be 2.
• If the person marked 5, the assigned score will be 1.

According to the COPSOQ II manual, in the Medium Version, the only items that undergo this inversion are 42 and 45. (In the Long Version, they correspond to items 51 and 54. The Short Version não has inverted items).

Why should it be done this way? Imagine we are measuring a positive factor such as "Trust." If the question is "Does management withhold information from its employees?" and the person answers "5 - Always," this indicates lack of trust. By applying mathematical inversion (transforming this 5 into a 1), we can align the response to speak the same numerical "language" as the other positive questions.

3 Calculation of Means per Factor and the Traffic Light Rule

Now that all questions have a standardized score from 1 to 5 (with the corrected items), it is time to group and interpret the results. The process works as follows:

Mean Calculation: You must group the questions belonging to the same subscale (e.g., "Burnout" block) and calculate the simple mean of the responses, summing them and dividing by the number of variables.

Attention to the Meaning of the Score: The meaning of the number changes depending on the theme. For example, a mean of 4 points in the factor "Possibilities for Development" represents an optimal situation. However, a mean of 4 points in the factor "Job Insecurity" represents a terrible risk situation.

Division into Tertiles (The Traffic Light Rule): To make risk visualization instantaneous and understandable, the score range (from 1 to 5) is mathematically divided into three equal parts (tertiles). The corporate cutoff points required by the literature are exactly 2,33 e 3,66.

  • GREEN (Favorable Situation): Scores less than or equal to 2,33. Healthy organizational practices.
  • YELLOW (Intermediate Situation): Scores between 2,34 e 3,65. Point of attention and mapping.
  • RED (Health Risk): Scores equal to or above 3,66. Acute sign that mitigating interventions are urgent in the company.
