About this tool

Early detection of signs of clinical deterioration in pregnant and postpartum women by scoring 9 physiological parameters: temperature, systolic and diastolic blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, supplemental oxygen use, level of consciousness, and urine output. Indicated for pregnant women at 20 weeks or more and postpartum women up to 6 weeks after delivery, for monitoring and early detection of clinical deterioration (e.g., severe preeclampsia, sepsis, hemorrhage).

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–0: No Change
  • 1–4: Alert - Increased Monitoring
  • 5–6: Increased Risk - Urgent Response
  • 7–26: Critical Risk - Immediate Response

Important tips

  • MEOWS is applied to pregnant women at 20 weeks or more and postpartum women up to 6 weeks after delivery.
  • A single parameter scoring 3 is sufficient to trigger an urgent response, regardless of the total score.
  • Normal physiological changes of pregnancy (e.g., slight increase in heart and respiratory rates) were considered when defining scoring thresholds.
  • Total scores equal to or greater than 7 require immediate activation of institutional rapid response and consideration of ICU support.

    Foundation

    MEOWS sums the scores of 9 physiological parameters — temperature, systolic and diastolic blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, supplemental oxygen use, level of consciousness (AVPU), and urine output — each scored according to the degree of deviation from normal, for early detection of clinical deterioration in pregnant and postpartum women.

    History

    Developed from Early Warning Scores used in general care, MEOWS was specifically adapted for the obstetric population, considering normal physiological changes of pregnancy, and has been adopted by various maternal health services worldwide.

    NANDA — Decreased cardiac output

    Inadequate amount of blood pumped by the heart to meet the body's metabolic demands, identified by changes in heart rate and blood pressure on MEOWS.

    NANDA — Ineffective breathing pattern

    Inspiration and/or expiration that does not provide adequate ventilation, identified by changes in respiratory rate and oxygen saturation.

    NANDA — Risk for shock

    Risk of inadequate blood flow to body tissues, which may lead to cellular dysfunction with risk of death, related to hemodynamic deterioration identified by the score.

    NOC — Vital signs

    • MEOWS systematically assessed per institutional protocol
    • Clinical deterioration identified and reported early
    • Clinical response escalated proportionally to the obtained score

    NIC — Vital signs monitoring in obstetrics

    • Apply MEOWS in pregnant women at 20 weeks or more and postpartum women up to 6 weeks after delivery
    • Sum the scores of all parameters to obtain the total score
    • Escalate monitoring frequency and clinical response according to the obtained score
    • Immediately notify the medical team if any single parameter scores 3 or the total score is ≥5
    • Activate institutional rapid response (obstetric emergency code) for scores ≥7

    Scientific Validation

    Widely used in maternity units and obstetric services as a screening tool for early detection of clinical deterioration, although evidence on the exact standardization of thresholds is still debated, with variations among different institutional versions.

    Limitations

    MEOWS trigger thresholds vary among institutions and are mostly based on clinical consensus, with limited evidence on its ability to improve outcomes in the general obstetric population. It should be used as a complementary tool to clinical judgment, not in isolation.

    How MEOWS works

    MEOWS sums the scores of 9 physiological parameters (temperature, SBP, DBP, HR, RR, SpO2, O2 use, consciousness, and urine output), with individual scores increasing as deviation from normal increases.

    Quick guide: 0 = Normal | 1-4 = Increased monitoring | 5-6 (or 3 in 1 parameter) = Urgent response | ≥7 = Immediate response/emergency code.

    Does MEOWS have internationally standardized thresholds?

    Not completely. MEOWS trigger thresholds vary across institutions and countries, being mostly based on clinical consensus. Therefore, it is important to follow the specific protocol adopted by the healthcare institution.

    Does MEOWS replace the clinical judgment of the team?

    No. MEOWS is a complementary screening and monitoring tool. If a patient or family member expresses concern about the clinical condition, the team should escalate care regardless of the numerical score obtained.
