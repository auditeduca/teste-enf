About this tool

Assess the risk of developing pressure injury, considering body build, continence, skin type, mobility, sex, age, appetite, and special risk factors. Indicated at hospital admission and periodically according to institutional protocol, as an alternative or complement to the Braden Scale.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–9: No Risk
  • 10–14: Risk
  • 15–19: High Risk
  • 20–99: Very High Risk

Important tips

  • The Waterlow Scale includes special risk factors (tissue malnutrition, neurological deficit, major surgery) not directly addressed by the Braden Scale.
  • Reassess the patient's skin at each position change, regardless of the calculated score.
  • Combine risk assessment with complete physical examination of the skin, especially over bony prominences.
  • The choice between Waterlow and Braden usually depends on the current institutional protocol.

    Foundation

    The Waterlow Scale was developed by Judy Waterlow in the United Kingdom, assessing multiple risk domains for pressure injury: body build, continence, skin type, mobility, sex, age, appetite, and special risk factors (tissue malnutrition, neurological deficit, major surgery/trauma, and risk medications).

    History

    Widely used in healthcare services in the United Kingdom and other countries as an alternative or complement to the Braden Scale, being considered more comprehensive as it includes special risk factors often not covered by other scales.

    NANDA — Risk for impaired skin integrity

    Susceptibility to alteration in the epidermis and/or dermis, which may compromise health.

    NANDA — Impaired physical mobility

    Limitation in independent voluntary movement of body parts or the whole body.

    NANDA — Imbalanced Nutrition: Less Than Body Requirements

    Insufficient nutrient intake to meet metabolic needs.

    NOC — Tissue integrity: skin and mucous membranes

    • Absence of pressure injury
    • Adequate tissue perfusion
    • Adequate skin hydration

    NIC — Pressure Injury Prevention

    • Apply validated risk scale (Waterlow)
    • Reposition patient according to identified risk
    • Use specialized support surfaces when indicated
    • Keep skin clean and hydrated
    • Assess and optimize nutritional status

    Scientific Validation

    Validation studies show good sensitivity, although with variable specificity depending on the study population, being a widely used tool in European clinical practice.

    Limitations

    Because it is more complex than the Braden Scale, with multiple subdomains (including special factors combined in this calculator into a single simplified selection), it may present greater variability in application among different assessors without adequate training.

    How the Waterlow Scale works

    The Waterlow Scale sums points from multiple domains — body build, continence, skin type, mobility, sex, age, appetite, and special risk factors — to estimate the risk of developing pressure injury.

    Quick guide: 0-9 = No risk | 10-14 = Risk | 15-19 = High risk | ≥20 = Very high risk.

    Should I use Waterlow or Braden?

    Both are validated scales for pressure injury risk assessment. The choice generally follows the current institutional protocol. Waterlow tends to be more sensitive (identifies more patients at risk), while Braden is more widely studied and used internationally.

    How often should the Waterlow Scale be reapplied?

    Reassessment is recommended on admission, at each significant change in clinical status, and according to the frequency defined by the institutional protocol (usually daily in at-risk patients).
