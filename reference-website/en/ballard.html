About this tool

Estimate gestational age of the newborn by assessing 6 neuromuscular maturity signs and 6 physical maturity signs. Indicated to estimate gestational age when last menstrual period is uncertain or early ultrasound is unavailable, ideally within the first 12-24 hours of life (valid up to 96 hours).

Interpretation ranges

  • -11–9: Extremamente Pré-termo (< 28 semanas)
  • 10–19: Very Preterm (28 to 31 weeks)
  • 20–32: Moderate to Late Preterm (32 to 36 weeks)
  • 33–50: Term (37 weeks or more)

Important tips

  • The New Ballard Score is most accurate when applied within the first 12-24 hours of life, being valid up to 96 hours after birth.
  • Each 5-point increment in the total score corresponds to approximately 2 additional weeks of estimated gestational age.
  • Genital assessment depends on the newborn's sex — select the corresponding option (male or female) from the combined list.
  • In critically ill newborns, neuromuscular assessment may be compromised by sedation or illness — give greater weight to physical maturity signs in these cases.

    Foundation

    The New Ballard Score sums the scores of 6 signs of neuromuscular maturity (posture, square window, arm recoil, popliteal angle, scarf sign, heel-to-ear) and 6 signs of physical maturity (skin, lanugo, plantar surface, breast, eye/ear, genitals), with a total score that is converted into estimated gestational age using a reference table (each 5-point increment corresponds to approximately 2 additional weeks of gestational age).

    History

    Developed by Jeanne Ballard and colleagues, the New Ballard Score (1991) is a revision and expansion of the original Dubowitz score, extending applicability to extremely preterm newborns (from 20 weeks).

    NANDA — Ineffective thermoregulation

    Temperature fluctuation between hypothermia and hyperthermia, with increased risk in newborns with lower physical maturity identified by Ballard.

    NANDA — Ineffective breathing pattern

    Inspiration and/or expiration that does not provide adequate ventilation, with risk proportional to the degree of prematurity.

    NANDA — Imbalanced nutrition: less than body requirements

    Insufficient nutrient intake to meet metabolic needs, relevant in preterm newborns with immature sucking reflex.

    NOC — Premature infant adaptation

    • Gestational age correctly estimated by New Ballard Score
    • Care plan adjusted according to identified degree of prematurity
    • Absence of complications of prematurity not detected early

    NIC — Preterm newborn care

    • Apply New Ballard Score ideally within the first 12-24 hours of life (valid up to 96 hours)
    • Correlate result with gestational age estimated by last menstrual period/ultrasound, when available
    • Adjust the care plan according to the identified degree of prematurity
    • Monitor signs of specific prematurity complications
    • Record the score and estimated gestational age in the medical record

    Scientific Validation

    Widely validated and used internationally for estimating gestational age at birth, with good correlation with gestational age calculated by last menstrual period and early ultrasound, especially when applied within the first 12-24 hours of life.

    Limitations

    Accuracy is highest when applied within the first 12-24 hours of life (valid up to 96 hours). Critically ill newborns or those with certain neurological conditions may have compromised neuromuscular assessment, requiring greater weight on physical maturity in the estimate.

    How the New Ballard Score Works

    The New Ballard Score sums the scores of 6 neuromuscular maturity signs and 6 physical maturity signs. The total score is converted into an estimated gestational age (each 5 points ≈ 2 additional weeks).

    Quick guide: Score <10 = Extremely preterm (<28 wks) | 10-19 = Very preterm (28-31 wks) | 20-32 = Moderate to late preterm (32-36 wks) | ≥33 = Term (≥37 wks).

    Does the New Ballard Score replace obstetric ultrasound for estimating gestational age?

    No. When available, early ultrasound (ideally in the first trimester) is considered more accurate. The New Ballard Score is especially useful when the last menstrual period date is uncertain or early ultrasound is unavailable.

    Can the score be applied at any time after birth?

    The New Ballard Score is most accurate when applied within the first 12-24 hours of life, and its validity is considered up to 96 hours after birth. After this period, physical and neuromuscular maturity may not accurately reflect the actual gestational age.
