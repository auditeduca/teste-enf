About this tool

Assess the risk of developing pressure injury based on 5 domains: mental status, continence, mobility, activity, and nutrition. Indicated for pressure injury risk assessment in hospitalized patients and long-term care, being an adaptation of the Norton Scale.

Interpretation ranges

  • 5–10: High Risk
  • 11–15: Moderate Risk
  • 16–20: Low Risk

Important tips

  • As with the Norton Scale, on the Gosnell Scale LOWER scores indicate HIGHER risk of pressure injury.
  • The scale sums 5 domains with different weights: mental status (1-5), continence/mobility/activity (1-4 each) and nutrition (1-3), totaling 5 to 20 points.
  • The original full assessment also includes description of skin appearance, tone, and sensitivity, in addition to vital signs — complementary to the numerical score.
  • It is considered an adaptation of the Norton Scale, with greater detail on mental status and the nutritional domain.

    Foundation

    The Gosnell Scale assesses 5 domains — mental status (1 to 5 points), continence, mobility and activity (1 to 4 points each) and nutrition (1 to 3 points) — totaling a score from 5 to 20 points, with lower scores indicating higher risk of pressure injury.

    History

    Developed by Davina J. Gosnell in 1973 and revised in 1989, it is an adaptation of the Norton Scale, incorporating a more detailed assessment of mental status and nutrition.

    NANDA — Risk for impaired tissue integrity

    Risk of damage to mucous, corneal, subcutaneous, muscle, cartilage, bone, or vascular tissue, related to risk factors identified by the Gosnell Scale.

    NANDA — Impaired physical mobility

    Limitation in independent, intentional physical movement of the body or one or more extremities.

    NANDA — Imbalanced nutrition: less than body requirements

    Insufficient nutrient intake to meet metabolic needs, relevant in patients with low scores in the nutrition domain.

    NOC — Tissue integrity: skin and mucous membranes

    • Gosnell score assessed and reassessed periodically
    • Intact skin, no signs of pressure injury
    • Prevention plan implemented according to identified risk

    NIC — Pressure ulcer prevention

    • Apply the Gosnell Scale on admission and reassess periodically
    • Perform repositioning according to identified risk and institutional protocol
    • Assess skin regularly, especially over bony prominences
    • Involve the nutrition team in cases of low scores in the nutritional domain
    • Record vital signs and skin characteristics (appearance, tone, sensitivity) according to institutional protocol

    Scientific Validation

    Comparative studies show good predictive validity of the Gosnell Scale in identifying patients at risk for pressure injury, with performance comparable to other established scales such as Norton and Braden.

    Limitations

    It is a simpler scale than Braden, with less detail on factors such as friction/shear. The original full scale also includes a descriptive assessment of the skin (appearance, tone, and sensitivity), which complements the numerical score.

    How the Gosnell Scale Works

    The Gosnell Scale sums the scores of 5 domains — mental status (1-5), continence, mobility, and activity (1-4 each) and nutrition (1-3) — totaling 5 to 20 points. Lower scores indicate higher risk.

    Quick guide: 5-10 = High risk | 11-15 = Moderate risk | 16-20 = Low risk.

    What is the difference between the Gosnell Scale and the Braden Scale?

    Both assess risk of pressure injury, but Braden assesses 6 domains with greater detail (including sensory perception and friction/shear), while Gosnell, adapted from Norton, assesses 5 domains with additional emphasis on mental status and nutrition.

    Is the numerical score of the Gosnell sufficient for a complete risk assessment?

    The original complete scale also recommends recording skin appearance, tone, and sensitivity, as well as vital signs, as supplementary information to the numerical score of the 5 domains.
