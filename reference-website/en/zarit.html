About this tool

Assess the degree of physical, emotional, and social burden perceived by the family caregiver of dependent, elderly, or chronically ill patients. Indicated for family caregivers of patients with functional dependence, dementia, or debilitating chronic diseases, in home, outpatient, or hospital follow-up.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–12: No Burden / Mild Burden
  • 13–24: Mild to Moderate Burden
  • 25–36: Moderate to Severe Burden
  • 37–48: Severe Burden

Important tips

  • Administer the scale in a private setting, without the patient present, to allow more honest responses from the caregiver.
  • High scores should prompt assessment of risk for burnout and caregiver illness.
  • Consider readministering the scale periodically, especially after changes in the patient's clinical condition.
  • Early identification of burden allows preventive interventions before a care crisis.

    Foundation

    The Zarit Burden Interview was developed by Steven Zarit and colleagues to assess perceived burden among family caregivers of elderly or dependent patients. The short version, with 12 items, was validated as a faster alternative to the original 22-item version, maintaining good discriminative ability.

    History

    Widely used in research and clinical practice in geriatrics, mental health, and palliative care to identify caregivers at risk of physical and emotional exhaustion, supporting interventions.

    NANDA — Caregiver role strain

    Difficulty in performing the family caregiver role.

    NANDA — Risk for caregiver role strain

    Susceptibility to difficulty in performing the family caregiver role, which may compromise health.

    NANDA — Compromised family coping

    Insufficient, ineffective, or compromised support, comfort, assistance, or encouragement usually provided by the reference person (family member or other caregiver).

    NOC — Caregiver resilience

    • Verbalizes feelings about the caregiving situation
    • Uses effective coping strategies
    • Maintains social support network

    NIC — Caregiver support

    • Assess caregiver burden level using a validated scale (Zarit)
    • Identify available support resources (family, community, formal)
    • Provide guidance on self-care strategies and burnout prevention
    • Refer to psychosocial support when indicated

    Scientific Validation

    The 12-item short version shows good correlation with the original 22-item version and adequate internal consistency in validation studies, including adaptations for the Brazilian population.

    Limitations

    Cutoff points may vary across validation studies. The scale assesses subjective perception of burden and should be complemented by a broader clinical evaluation of the caregiver's family and social context.

    How the Short Zarit Burden Interview works

    The Short Zarit Burden Interview sums scores from 12 items about the caregiver's perception of the impact of caring for a dependent family member, each scored from 0 (never) to 4 (always), totaling 0 to 48 points.

    Quick guide: 0-12 = Absent/mild | 13-24 = Mild to moderate | 25-36 = Moderate to severe | 37-48 = Severe.

    Should the Zarit Burden Interview be administered to the patient or the caregiver?

    To the primary family caregiver, assessing their personal perception of the impact of caring for the patient on their life, health, and social relationships.

    Does a high score on the Zarit Burden Interview mean the caregiver is doing a poor job?

    No. The scale measures perceived burden, not quality of care. A high score indicates the caregiver needs more support and attention to their own physical and mental health, regardless of the quality of care provided.

    This tool is for educational and clinical decision support purposes. Results should always be reviewed and validated by a qualified nursing professional, according to patient assessment and institutional protocols.
