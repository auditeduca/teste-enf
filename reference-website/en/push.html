About this tool

Objectively monitor the evolution of pressure injury healing over time, based on 3 parameters: wound area, exudate amount, and predominant tissue type in the wound bed. Indicated for serial reassessment of existing pressure injuries, allowing objective tracking of whether the injury is healing, stable, or worsening.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–0: Healed Wound
  • 1–9: Favorable Evolution
  • 10–17: Extensive Wound — Monitor Evolution

Important tips

  • Always reassess using the same measurement technique to ensure comparability between evaluations.
  • The most important aspect is the score trend over time, not the isolated value of a single assessment.
  • Record standardized photographs along with the score for more complete documentation.
  • Investigate causes of stagnation or worsening of the score (nutrition, perfusion, persistent pressure, infection).

    Foundation

    The PUSH Tool was developed by the National Pressure Ulcer Advisory Panel (NPUAP) as a simple and standardized instrument to monitor the evolution of pressure injury healing over time, assessing area, exudate, and tissue type.

    History

    Widely used in wound and ostomy care services to objectively document the clinical evolution of pressure injuries, allowing standardized comparison between serial assessments.

    NANDA — Impaired Skin Integrity

    Alteration in epidermis and/or dermis.

    NANDA — Impaired Tissue Integrity

    Damage to mucous, corneal, subcutaneous, muscle, cartilage, bone, or vascular tissue.

    NANDA — Risk for infection

    Increased susceptibility to invasion by pathogenic organisms, related to skin discontinuity.

    NOC — Wound Healing: Secondary Intention

    • Decrease in wound size
    • Formation of granulation tissue
    • Reduction of exudate
    • Absence of signs of infection

    NIC — Wound Care

    • Measure and document area, exudate, and tissue type at each assessment
    • Perform dressing according to protocol and wound characteristics
    • Monitor for signs of infection
    • Record the evolution of the PUSH score over time
    • Provide guidance on repositioning and pressure relief

    Scientific Validation

    Validation studies demonstrate good sensitivity to detect changes over time, being a reliable tool for monitoring healing trends, although it does not replace a complete clinical wound assessment.

    Limitations

    The PUSH Tool was designed to monitor trends over time (progressive score reduction indicates healing), not to classify severity at a single isolated moment. It does not assess wound depth or undermining/tunneling.

    How the PUSH Tool works

    The PUSH Tool sums points from 3 parameters — wound area (0-10 points), amount of exudate (0-3 points), and predominant tissue type (0-4 points) — totaling 0 to 17 points. The value should be tracked over serial assessments: a progressive reduction in the score indicates favorable healing evolution.

    Quick guide: 0 = Healed wound | Lower scores = favorable trend | Higher scores = more extensive wound, requires closer monitoring.

    How often should the PUSH be reapplied?

    Weekly reassessment is recommended, or according to institutional protocol, to monitor the healing trend over time.

    Does a low PUSH score always mean a small and simple wound?

    Not necessarily in isolation — the value should be interpreted together with the trend of previous assessments and the patient's overall clinical context.

    Important Notice

    This tool is for educational and clinical decision support purposes. Results should always be reviewed and validated by a qualified nursing professional, according to patient assessment and institutional protocols.
