Advanced Cardiovascular Life Support (ACLS) - AHA Protocols and Guidelines

Advanced Cardiovascular Life Support (ACLS) according to the AHA

Systematic approach, treatment management, and updated guidelines for managing cardiac arrest and other cardiovascular emergencies. It is important to note that Advanced Life Support covers both out-of-hospital and in-hospital settings; depending on the situation involving cardiac arrest, some procedures change.

O Advanced Cardiovascular Life Support (ACLS) consists of a set of clinical procedures and protocols established by the American Heart Association (AHA) for the urgent treatment of cardiac arrest, lethal arrhythmias, stroke, and acute coronary syndromes. The central goal is to restore spontaneous circulation and preserve intact neurological function, being an essential skill for nurses in prehospital care units, intensive care units, and emergency departments.

O Advanced Cardiovascular Life Support (ACLS) is a set of evidence-based guidelines for the management of severe cardiovascular emergencies.

• What it is: Advanced management of cardiac arrest, arrhythmias, myocardial infarction, and stroke, using airway management, medications, and defibrillation.

• Year of creation and entity: Created in 1974 by the American Heart Association (AHA).

• Periodic review: Updated periodically, with major revisions every 5 years.

• Who can take it? Aimed at physicians, nurses, and emergency professionals.

• What are Algorithms? They are flowchart protocols that guide rapid and organized action.

ACLS certification is valid for 2 years, requiring recertification.

American Heart Association: https://www.heart.org/

Recognition of Clinical Deterioration and Prevention

Studies show that most in-hospital cardiac arrests (IHCA) are not sudden and unexpected events, but rather the final result of progressive physiological deterioration that can last hours. Early identification of these signs is the first link in the in-hospital Chain of Survival.

The Nurse's Role: Continuous monitoring and correct interpretation of vital signs are essential. The use of early warning scores standardizes communication.

  • NEWS 2 Protocol (National Early Warning Score): A tool that scores parameters such as respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, temperature, systolic blood pressure, heart rate, and level of consciousness.
  • Rapid Response Team (RRT): Multidisciplinary team (physician, nurse, physical therapist) activated to intervene at the bedside upon signs of deterioration (e.g., NEWS ≥ 5 or 7), preventing progression to cardiac arrest.

Recognition and Initial Management of Cardiac Arrest

If prevention fails and the patient collapses, immediate recognition is vital. Assessment should be performed in less than 10 seconds.

Critical BLS Steps in ACLS:

  1. Check Responsiveness: Call the patient and stimulate (tap shoulders).
  2. Call for Help: Activate the code blue/emergency team and request the Crash Cart/Defibrillator.
  3. Check Pulse and Breathing: Check carotid pulse and breathing (gasping or absent) simultaneously for no more than 10 seconds.
  4. Start High-Quality CPR: If no pulse, begin chest compressions (100-120/min, 5-6 cm depth).

Nurse's role during cardiac arrest/Code Blue:

The nurse must never leave the patient's room during cardiac arrest. Initially, their role is to delegate appropriately to distribute workload and ensure full coverage of patient care.

The nurse asks for help outside the room from other technicians, requests the presence of the physician and respiratory therapist, and also asks for the patient's chart and medical orders.

Simultaneously, performs initial decision-making with the team:

  • Ask to start the timer and announce every 2 minutes out loud
  • Turn on the cardioverter and set it to monitor mode
  • Ensure intubation kit is functional near the patient's head + medications;
  • Request setup of vacuum circuit and upper airway suctioning + bag-valve-mask ventilation;
  • Ensure large-bore IV access + draw blood and arterial blood gas;
  • Leave drawn epinephrine labeled, covered, and available + saline syringe;
  • Delegate two people for chest compressions;
  • Keep calcium gluconate, magnesium, potassium, and insulin sealed, nearby, and available;
  • Request portable warmer;
  • Request turning off the air conditioning in the room;
  • Request warming of normal saline solutions;
  • After adjusting the flow of care, take on a fixed task.

There is much for the nurse to do during cardiac arrest. The nurse never leaves the patient's room.

Primary ACLS Assessment (ABCDE)

Performed while the high-performance team executes CPR. The focus is to ensure effective resuscitation and basic physiological support. It is important to note that in Basic Life Support, the ABCDE sequence will be strictly followed in these steps; however, in hospitalized patients, Circulation (C) through chest compressions upon evidence of cardiac arrest will be initiated immediately.

  • A (Airway) - Airway

    Maintain patent airway, chin lift, orotracheal suctioning. In cardiac arrest, advanced airway with endotracheal tube is crucial. The nursing technician should check and test laryngoscope blades and ensure the laryngoscope handle batteries are not depleted. This equipment, along with endotracheal tubes, sterile gloves, stylet, bougie, suction system ready, and bag-valve-mask connected to oxygen, should be near the patient's head, and the patient's bed should be away from the wall.

  • B (Breathing) - Breathing

    Administer 100% oxygen via bag-valve-mask. Avoid excessive ventilation (may reduce venous return). If advanced airway is inserted: 1 ventilation every 6 seconds (10/min) without interrupting compressions.

  • C (Circulation) - Circulation

    The recommended depth for chest compressions in adults during CPR is at least 2 inches (5 cm), not exceeding 2.4 inches (6 cm). The rate should be 100 to 120 compressions per minute, ensuring full chest recoil. Rate: 100 to 120 compressions per minute (Use the multiparameter monitor to check if the rate is effective, never use the pulse oximeter as a parameter). Ensure IV/IO access. Monitor CPR quality (Capnography > 10 mmHg). Defibrillate if indicated (VT and VF), and in case of Asystole or PEA, administer epinephrine IV immediately along with chest compressions.

  • D (Disability) - Neurological

    Apply Glasgow Coma Scale. Check capillary blood glucose to rule out hypoglycemia.

  • E (Exposure) - Exposure

    Expose the chest for CPR and pads. Look for signs of trauma, bleeding, fistulas, or medical devices.

Secondary Assessment (Differential Diagnosis)

Consists of active search for probable causes of cardiac arrest. While the leader runs the code, a team member should collect the focused history using the secondary assessment (AMPLE). Have the patient's chart and latest lab results on hand.

AMPLE Mnemonic

  • A
    Allergies Check if the patient has drug or food allergies, or any reaction to medication.
  • M
    Medications Check the medical prescription for medications the patient is taking, last dose, complaints when administering medications; if out-of-hospital, possible intoxications should also be investigated (e.g., beta-blockers, opioids).
  • P
    Past Medical History Cardiac history, renal, diabetes, recent surgeries, depression treatment, drug use.
  • L
    Liquids / Ingestion Last meal (risk of aspiration), hydration status, and possible toxic liquids ingested by the patient.
  • A
    Environment / Events Observing the surrounding environment is extremely important; check around: empty pill strips, blunt cutting tools, firearms, illicit substances, etc. What happened before the collapse? (Chest pain, trauma, fever) Check with the companion or nursing technician and read the nursing notes; if out-of-hospital, gather information from any family member present.

Cardiac Arrest Algorithm: Shockable Rhythms

Occurs when there is chaotic electrical activity (VF) or organized but too fast (pulseless VT). Definitive treatment is electrical.

Ventricular Fibrillation (VF) / Pulseless VT

  • Immediate Defibrillation: Maximum energy (120-200J biphasic or 360J monophasic). "Shock, clear, shock delivered."
  • CPR (2 min): Resume compressions immediately after shock. Do not check pulse immediately after shock.
  • Epinephrine (1 mg): Every 3-5 min (after 2nd shock).
  • Amiodarone: Antiarrhythmic of choice for refractory VF/pulseless VT (after 3rd shock). Loading dose: 300 mg bolus. Repeat dose: 150 mg.
  • Lidocaine: Alternative to Amiodarone (1-1.5 mg/kg).

Cardiac Arrest Algorithm: Non-Shockable Rhythms

Includes Asystole (flat line) and PEA (any organized rhythm without palpable pulse). Shock does not work here.

Asystole / PEA

  • Immediate CPR: Focus on high-quality compressions and minimal interruptions. Compressions are paused every 3 minutes briefly to check central pulse; if absent, resume chest compressions. The nurse delegates 2 nursing technicians to alternate compressions every 3 minutes.
  • DO NOT SHOCK: Shock only injures the myocardium without reversing the rhythm.
  • Epinephrine (1 mg): The nurse delegates or takes over the crash cart. Maximum priority on drawing up epinephrine. Administer as soon as peripheral IV access is obtained. Repeat every 3-5 minutes. Increases coronary perfusion pressure. The person responsible for the crash cart during the code blue times the duration since the start of the arrest and announces aloud every 3 minutes.
  • Advanced Airway (Intubation): The physician is responsible for early orotracheal intubation to improve ventilation and capnography, but for this it is important that the nursing technician, nurse, or respiratory therapist suction the patient's airway before intubation. The respiratory therapist is responsible for setting up the mechanical ventilator and bagging until the ventilator circuit is connected.
  • Straight Line Protocol (CA-GA-DA) "It's not a trick" CABOS GAGAIN/CALIBRATION DALEAD: Before confirming asystole, check:
    • 1. Cables connected?
    • 2. Gain set to maximum?
    • 3. Leads (Check in more than one, e.g., II and V1).
    • 3. There may be electrode interference, damaged cable, or electrode detachment. Ensure everything is functioning correctly to avoid false alarms.

Differential Diagnosis: Reversible Causes

During CPR cycles, the team should mentally and verbally review the 5 Hs and 5 Ts. Treating the root cause is often the only way to achieve successful CPR.

The 5 Hs

  • 1. Hypovolemia: Volume loss (blood or fluids). Signs: High HR pre-arrest, hypotension. Treatment: Rapid fluids, blood.
  • 2. Hypoxia: Lack of oxygen. Treatment: Effective ventilation, 100% O2, endotracheal tube.
  • 3. Hydrogen (Acidosis): Sepsis, ketoacidosis. Treatment: Sodium bicarbonate (in specific cases), controlled hyperventilation.
  • 4. Hypo/Hyperkalemia: Potassium disorders. HyperK treatment: Calcium Chloride/Gluconate, Insulin+Glucose. HypoK: K+ and Magnesium replacement.
  • 5. Hypothermia: Cold patient. Heart does not respond well to drugs/shock. Treatment: Active internal and external rewarming.

The 5 Ts

  • 1. Tension (Pneumothorax): Hypertensive lung collapse. Signs: Tracheal deviation, absent breath sounds. Treatment: Needle decompression (thoracocentesis).
  • 2. Tamponade (Cardiac): Fluid in pericardium. Signs: Beck's triad (Hypotension, JVD, Muffled heart sounds). Treatment: Pericardiocentesis.
  • 3. Toxins: Drug overdose (opioids, tricyclics). Treatment: Specific antidotes (Naloxone, etc.) and supportive care.
  • 4. Thrombosis (Pulmonary): Massive embolism. Treatment: Thrombolysis (fibrinolytics) or embolectomy.
  • 5. Thrombosis (Coronary): Acute MI. Most common cause. Treatment: Immediate PCI post-ROSC.

Post-Resuscitation Care (Post-Cardiac Arrest)

Return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC) is not the end, but the beginning of a critical stabilization phase to mitigate secondary brain injury and multi-organ dysfunction.

Targeted Temperature Management (TTM): Maintain core temperature between 32°C and 36°C for at least 24 hours in patients not following commands. This reduces cerebral metabolism and inflammation.
Ventilation and Oxygenation: Avoid hyperoxia (causes free radicals). Maintain SpO2 92-98% and PaCO2 normal (35-45 mmHg).
Hemodynamics: Trate a hipotensão agressivamente com fluidos e vasopressores (Norepinefrina) para manter Blood Pressure Média (PAM) > 65 mmHg.
