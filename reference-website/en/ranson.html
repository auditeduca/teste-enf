About this tool

Estimate the severity and mortality risk of acute pancreatitis based on clinical and laboratory criteria assessed on admission and within the first 48 hours. Indicated in patients diagnosed with acute pancreatitis for early prognostic stratification.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–2: Mild Pancreatitis
  • 3–4: Moderate Pancreatitis
  • 5–6: Severe Pancreatitis
  • 7–11: Extremely Severe Pancreatitis

Important tips

  • Ranson criteria require two assessments: one on admission (5 criteria) and another at 48 hours (6 additional criteria).
  • The complete score is only available after 48 hours, which is a limitation compared to scales like BISAP and APACHE II.
  • Each positive criterion adds 1 point; the result is the total number of positive criteria, not a sum of numerical values.
  • Consider associating Ranson criteria with other prognostic scores (BISAP, APACHE II) for a more complete and early assessment.

    Foundation

    Ranson criteria evaluate 11 clinical and laboratory parameters: 5 on admission (age, leukocytes, glucose, LDH, and AST) and 6 at 48 hours (hematocrit drop, BUN increase, calcium, PaO2, base deficit, and fluid sequestration), summing the number of positive criteria to estimate severity and mortality of acute pancreatitis.

    History

    Developed by John Ranson in 1974, originally for alcoholic pancreatitis (with a slightly different set of criteria for biliary pancreatitis), they were one of the first objective prognostic systems for acute pancreatitis.

    NANDA — Deficient Fluid Volume

    Decrease in intravascular, interstitial, and/or intracellular fluid, related to fluid sequestration and systemic inflammatory response in severe pancreatitis.

    NANDA — Acute pain

    Unpleasant sensory and emotional experience associated with actual or potential tissue damage, related to pancreatic inflammation.

    NANDA — Risk for infection

    Increased susceptibility to invasion by pathogenic organisms, related to pancreatic necrosis and possible secondary infection.

    NANDA — Ineffective breathing pattern

    Inspiration and/or expiration that does not provide adequate ventilation, related to systemic complications of severe pancreatitis (e.g., acute respiratory distress syndrome).

    NOC — Severity of Systemic Infection/Inflammation

    • Ranson criteria assessed on admission and at 48 hours
    • Vital signs and organ function monitored
    • Absence or control of systemic complications

    NIC — Pancreatitis Care

    • Collect laboratory tests necessary for calculating Ranson criteria on admission and at 48 hours
    • Monitor vital signs, urine output, and respiratory function continuously
    • Assist with volume replacement as prescribed
    • Immediately report results with elevated criteria to the medical team
    • Assess need for transfer to intensive care unit

    Scientific Validation

    Widely used historically, although newer systems such as APACHE II and BISAP have the advantage of being fully calculable on admission without needing to wait 48 hours.

    Limitations

    Requires waiting 48 hours for complete calculation, delaying full risk stratification. Some laboratory parameters (base deficit, fluid sequestration) may not be routinely available in all services. More modern scales (BISAP, APACHE II) are fully calculable on admission.

    How Ranson Criteria Work

    Ranson Criteria sum the number of positive criteria among 11 parameters: 5 assessed on admission (age, leukocytes, glucose, LDH, AST) and 6 assessed at 48 hours (hematocrit drop, increase in BUN, calcium, PaO2, base deficit, fluid sequestration).

    Quick guide: 0-2 = Mild | 3-4 = Moderate | 5-6 = Severe | ≥7 = Extremely severe.

    Are Ranson criteria specific to any type of pancreatitis?

    They were originally described for alcoholic pancreatitis. There is a variation of the criteria for biliary pancreatitis, with slight differences in cutoff values.

    Why do I need to wait 48 hours for the full score?

    Six of the eleven criteria (hematocrit drop, increase in BUN, calcium, PaO2, base deficit, and fluid sequestration) can only be assessed by comparing with admission values after 48 hours of clinical evolution.
