About this tool

Assess the severity of community-acquired pneumonia and guide the decision on need for hospital admission or outpatient treatment. Indicated for adult patients diagnosed with community-acquired pneumonia in the emergency department or primary care.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–1: Low Risk
  • 2–2: Moderate Risk
  • 3–5: High Risk

Important tips

  • CRB-65 (without urea) can be used in primary care when laboratory tests are not readily available.
  • Always consider social factors and associated comorbidities in the final decision, in addition to the numerical score.
  • Scores 4-5 suggest evaluation for intensive care, not just ward admission.
  • Reassess treatment response within the first 48-72 hours.

    Foundation

    CURB-65 was developed by Lim et al., validated and recommended by the British Thoracic Society, assessing 5 criteria: Confusion, Urea, Respiratory rate, Blood pressure, and age ≥65 years.

    History

    Widely used in emergency departments to stratify severity of community-acquired pneumonia and guide the decision between outpatient treatment and hospital admission.

    NANDA — Ineffective breathing pattern

    Inspiration and/or expiration that does not provide adequate ventilation.

    NANDA — Risk for infection

    Increased susceptibility to invasion by pathogenic organisms.

    NANDA — Impaired gas exchange

    Excess or deficit in oxygenation and/or carbon dioxide elimination at the alveolar-capillary membrane.

    NOC — Respiratory status

    • Respiratory rate within expected limits
    • Absence of confusion
    • Stable vital signs

    NIC — Respiratory monitoring

    • Assess respiratory rate and breathing pattern
    • Monitor level of consciousness
    • Check blood pressure regularly
    • Report elevated score to medical team
    • Assist with laboratory tests (BUN)

    Scientific Validation

    Validated in multiple international studies, with good correlation between score and 30-day mortality. A simplified version without urea measurement (CRB-65) can be used in primary care when lab tests are unavailable.

    Limitations

    Should not be the sole criterion for hospitalization decision — factors such as hypoxemia, decompensated comorbidities, social conditions, and ability to adhere to outpatient treatment should also be considered.

    How CURB-65 works

    CURB-65 adds 1 point for each of the 5 criteria present: Confusion, elevated Urea, Respiratory rate ≥30 breaths/min, low Blood pressure, and age ≥65 years, totaling 0 to 5 points.

    Quick guide: 0-1 = Low risk (outpatient) | 2 = Moderate risk (consider admission) | 3-5 = High risk (admission, consider ICU if 4-5).

    Does CURB-65 replace clinical judgment?

    No. It is a decision support tool that should be combined with individualized clinical assessment, including hypoxemia, comorbidities, and the patient's social conditions.

    What is the difference between CURB-65 and CRB-65?

    CRB-65 is a simplified version without serum urea measurement, useful in settings without rapid access to laboratory tests, such as primary care.
