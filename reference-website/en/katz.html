About this tool

Assess the patient's degree of functional independence in 6 basic activities of daily living (ADLs). Indicated for elderly patients, those in rehabilitation, or any context of functional capacity assessment for basic self-care activities.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–2: Severe Dependence
  • 3–4: Moderate Dependence
  • 5–6: Independence

Important tips

  • Assess the patient's actual performance, not their theoretical ability.
  • Combine with the Lawton Scale to assess instrumental activities (IADLs).
  • Reassess after clinical events or changes in functional status.
  • Always consider the patient's home/social context in the interpretation.

    Foundation

    The Katz Index was developed by Sidney Katz and colleagues in 1963 to assess functional independence of elderly individuals in 6 basic activities of daily living: bathing, dressing, toileting, transfer, continence, and feeding.

    History

    One of the most widely used instruments in geriatrics and gerontology for functional assessment, serving as a basis for many subsequent functional capacity scales.

    NANDA — Self-care deficit: bathing

    Impaired ability to perform or complete bathing/hygiene activities by oneself.

    NANDA — Impaired physical mobility

    Limitation in independent voluntary movement of body parts or the whole body.

    NOC — Self-care: activities of daily living

    • Performs personal hygiene
    • Dresses without help
    • Feeds without help
    • Bowel and bladder control

    NIC — Self-care assistance

    • Assess functional capacity
    • Encourage residual independence
    • Adapt environment as needed
    • Involve caregiver/family in care plan

    Scientific Validation

    Extensively validated in hospitalized, institutionalized, and community-dwelling elderly populations, with good interobserver reliability.

    Limitations

    Assesses only basic activities of daily living (ADLs), not instrumental activities (IADLs) such as telephone use, financial management, or meal preparation—for these, the Lawton Scale is recommended.

    How the Katz Index works

    The Katz Index assesses 6 basic activities of daily living — bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring, continence, and feeding — classifying each as independent (1 point) or dependent (0 points). The sum ranges from 0 to 6 points.

    Quick guide: 5-6 = Independence | 3-4 = Moderate dependence | 0-2 = Severe dependence.

    What is the difference between the Katz Index and the Lawton Scale?

    The Katz assesses basic activities of daily living (bathing, dressing, hygiene, transferring, continence, feeding), while the Lawton Scale assesses more complex instrumental activities (telephone, shopping, finances, transportation, medication).

    Can the Katz Index be used at any age?

    It was developed and validated mainly for the elderly population, but can be applied to any adult with suspected functional impairment.
