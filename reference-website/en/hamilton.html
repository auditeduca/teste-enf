About this tool

Assess the severity of anxiety symptoms, both psychic and somatic, through 14 items rated by the interviewer. Indicated for assessing the severity of anxious symptoms in adults with anxiety disorders and for monitoring treatment response over time.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–17: Mild Anxiety
  • 18–24: Mild to Moderate Anxiety
  • 25–30: Moderate to Severe Anxiety
  • 31–56: Severe Anxiety

Important tips

  • The HAM-A is a hetero-assessment scale (administered by a trained interviewer), not a self-report questionnaire.
  • It assesses both psychic symptoms (anxious mood, tension, fears) and somatic symptoms (cardiovascular, respiratory, gastrointestinal, among others).
  • Somatic symptoms may overlap with general medical conditions — always consider the complete clinical context before attributing symptoms solely to anxiety.
  • Reapply the scale periodically to monitor response to pharmacological and/or psychotherapeutic treatment.

    Foundation

    The HAM-A assesses 14 items — anxious mood, tension, fears, insomnia, intellectual functions, depressed mood, muscular and sensory somatic symptoms, cardiovascular symptoms, respiratory symptoms, gastrointestinal symptoms, genitourinary symptoms, autonomic symptoms, and behavior during interview — each scored from 0 to 4, totaling 0 to 56 points.

    History

    Developed by Max Hamilton in 1959, it was one of the first standardized scales for assessing the severity of anxiety symptoms, widely used in clinical research and practice to this day.

    NANDA — Anxiety

    Vague and uncomfortable feeling of discomfort or fear, accompanied by autonomic response, identified by psychic and somatic symptoms assessed on the scale.

    NANDA — Disturbed sleep pattern

    Time-limited interruptions in the quantity and quality of sleep, related to the insomnia item of the scale.

    NANDA — Ineffective Coping

    Inability to form a valid appraisal of stressors, inadequate choices of practical responses, and/or inability to use available resources.

    NOC — Anxiety self-control

    • HAM-A score assessed and monitored throughout treatment
    • Specialized referral performed according to identified severity
    • Reduction in HAM-A score after therapeutic interventions

    NIC — Anxiety reduction

    • Administer the HAM-A through a structured clinical interview
    • Refer for psychiatric/psychological evaluation according to identified severity
    • Provide guidance on relaxation techniques and stress management
    • Monitor somatic symptoms that may mimic other clinical conditions
    • Reapply the scale periodically to monitor response to pharmacological and/or psychotherapeutic treatment

    Scientific Validation

    Widely validated internationally as a hetero-assessment instrument (administered by the interviewer) for measuring the severity of anxiety symptoms and monitoring treatment response.

    Limitations

    It is a hetero-assessment scale, requiring rater training for reliable application. Somatic symptoms may overlap with general medical conditions, requiring careful clinical evaluation to differentiate anxiety from organic causes.

    How the Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale (HAM-A) works

    The HAM-A sums the score (0 to 4) of 14 items assessing psychic and somatic symptoms of anxiety, totaling 0 to 56 points.

    Quick guide: 0-17 = Mild | 18-24 = Mild to moderate | 25-30 = Moderate to severe | 31-56 = Severe.

    What is the difference between the HAM-A and the GAD-7?

    The GAD-7 is a brief self-report questionnaire (7 items), while the HAM-A is a more extensive scale (14 items) of hetero-assessment, administered by a trained interviewer, and evaluates somatic symptoms in more detail in addition to psychic symptoms.

    Can the HAM-A be used to diagnose an anxiety disorder?

    No. The HAM-A assesses the severity of anxiety symptoms and is useful for monitoring treatment response, but the diagnosis of a specific anxiety disorder requires a complete clinical evaluation with formal diagnostic criteria (e.g., DSM-5 or ICD-11).
