About this tool

Screen for delirium based on 4 characteristic clinical criteria, enabling early identification and timely intervention. Indicated for hospitalized patients, especially older adults, postoperative and critically ill patients with suspected acute change in mental status.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–0: CAM Negative
  • 1–1: CAM Positive — Delirium Present

Important tips

  • Criteria 1 and 2 are mandatory; diagnosis also requires at least one of criteria 3 or 4.
  • Always compare with the patient's baseline mental status, not just the current isolated state.
  • In mechanically ventilated or non-verbal patients, use the adapted CAM-ICU version.
  • CAM should only be applied in patients with RASS between -3 and +4 (level of consciousness compatible with assessment).

    Foundation

    The CAM (Confusion Assessment Method) was developed by Sharon Inouye and colleagues in 1990, being the most widely used and validated delirium screening instrument worldwide. It is based on 4 criteria: (1) acute onset and fluctuating course, (2) inattention, (3) disorganized thinking, and (4) altered level of consciousness. Diagnosis requires the presence of criteria 1 and 2, plus at least one of criteria 3 or 4.

    History

    Adapted for the intensive care environment as CAM-ICU, allowing assessment in non-verbal and mechanically ventilated patients, recommended by international guidelines for pain, agitation, and delirium management (PADIS).

    NANDA — Acute Confusion

    Abrupt onset of reversible disturbances of consciousness, attention, cognition, and perception that develop over a short period.

    NANDA — Risk for falls

    Increased vulnerability to falls, which may cause physical harm, associated with confusion.

    NOC — Acute Confusion Level

    • Orientation to time, place, and person preserved
    • Attention maintained during interaction
    • Organized and coherent thinking
    • Normal level of consciousness

    NIC — Delirium Management

    • Apply the CAM systematically in at-risk patients
    • Identify and correct precipitating factors (pain, infection, metabolic disorders, polypharmacy)
    • Promote reorientation measures (clock, calendar, presence of family members)
    • Ensure adequate sleep-wake cycle
    • Communicate findings to the medical team for investigation and management

    Scientific Validation

    It presents sensitivity between 94% and 100% and specificity between 90% and 95% in original and validation studies, when applied by trained raters.

    Sensitivity: 94% · Specificity: 90%

    Limitations

    Requires adequate team training for reliable application. The standard CAM assumes verbal communication; for non-verbal or mechanically ventilated patients, the adapted CAM-ICU version should be used.

    How CAM works

    CAM assesses 4 clinical criteria: acute onset/fluctuating course, inattention, disorganized thinking, and altered level of consciousness. The result is positive for delirium when criteria 1 and 2 are present, plus at least one of criteria 3 or 4.

    Quick guide: Criteria 1 + 2 + (3 or 4) = CAM Positive (delirium present). Absence of this combination = CAM Negative.

    Can CAM be applied at any level of consciousness?

    No. CAM (and CAM-ICU) should only be applied when the patient has a level of consciousness compatible with assessment, generally RASS between -3 and +4. Deep sedation (RASS -4 or -5) precludes reliable assessment.

    Is delirium the same as dementia?

    No. Delirium has acute onset and fluctuating course, generally reversible when the underlying cause is treated. Dementia has insidious onset, progressive and chronic course. Both can coexist, making differential diagnosis difficult.
