About this tool

Assess the degree of frailty in older adults through 9 clinical and functional domains, aiding in care planning and identifying patients at higher risk for adverse outcomes. Indicated in comprehensive geriatric assessment, especially in older adults in outpatient consultations, preoperative evaluation, or before relevant therapeutic decisions.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–5: Not Frail / Vigorous
  • 6–7: Apparently Vulnerable
  • 8–9: Mild Frailty
  • 10–11: Moderate Frailty
  • 12–17: Severe Frailty

Important tips

  • The EFS assesses 9 different domains, offering a multidimensional view of frailty beyond purely physical aspects.
  • The functional performance component uses the Timed Up and Go test, which can be timed during the assessment itself.
  • Frailty is not synonymous with dependence — frail older adults may still be independent but with greater vulnerability to stressful events.
  • Use the result to guide referrals: mild to moderate frailty generally benefits from comprehensive geriatric assessment and multiprofessional interventions.

    Foundation

    The Edmonton Frail Scale assesses 9 domains — cognition, general health status, functional independence, social support, medication use, nutrition, mood, continence, and functional performance — summing scores from 0 to 2 in each domain, for a total score of 0 to 17 points.

    History

    Developed by Rolfson and colleagues at the University of Edmonton (Canada), it is one of the most widely used frailty screening tools due to its multidimensional approach and rapid applicability in different clinical contexts.

    NANDA — Frail elderly syndrome

    Dynamic and nonlinear clinical state of instability that increases the risk of functional decline and/or physical and/or cognitive health decline, increased vulnerability, and decreased recovery capacity after a stressful event.

    NANDA — Risk for falls

    Increased susceptibility to falls, which may cause physical harm, related to functional and cognitive impairment identified in the frailty assessment.

    NANDA — Risk for imbalanced nutrition: less than body requirements

    Risk of insufficient nutrient intake to meet metabolic needs, related to identified unintentional weight loss.

    NOC — Frailty status

    • Frailty score assessed and monitored over time
    • Individualized care plan implemented according to frailty level
    • Adequate social and family support identified or provided

    NIC — Geriatric care

    • Apply the Edmonton Frail Scale in the comprehensive geriatric assessment
    • Refer for multiprofessional evaluation according to identified frailty level
    • Review polypharmacy and simplify medication regimens when possible
    • Assess and strengthen the older adult's social support network
    • Monitor weight, mood, and functional performance periodically

    Scientific Validation

    Validated in various clinical contexts and populations, including preoperative assessment, showing good correlation with adverse outcomes such as hospitalization, institutionalization, and mortality.

    Limitations

    Some items depend on subjective assessment (e.g., health perception, mood) and patient collaboration. The scale screens for frailty but does not replace a complete comprehensive geriatric assessment for definitive therapeutic planning.

    How does the Edmonton Frail Scale work?

    The EFS sums scores (0 to 2) across 9 domains: cognition, general health status, functional independence, social support, medication use, nutrition, mood, continence, and functional performance (Timed Up and Go), totaling 0 to 17 points.

    Quick guide: 0-5 = Not frail | 6-7 = Vulnerable | 8-9 = Mild frailty | 10-11 = Moderate frailty | 12-17 = Severe frailty.

    Does the Edmonton Frail Scale replace the comprehensive geriatric assessment?

    No. It is a rapid screening tool that helps identify older adults at higher risk, directing which patients would benefit from a more detailed comprehensive geriatric assessment.

    Is frailty the same as advanced old age?

    No. Frailty is a clinical syndrome characterized by increased vulnerability to stressors, which can occur at different advanced ages and is not present in all older adults, even the very old.
