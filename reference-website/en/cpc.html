About this tool

Classify the patient's functional neurological outcome after cardiac arrest and resuscitation on a standardized scale from 1 (good performance) to 5 (death/brain death). Indicated for survivors of cardiac arrest, for neurological outcome assessment during hospitalization and post-discharge follow-up.

Interpretation ranges

  • 1–1: Good Cerebral Performance
  • 2–2: Moderate Cerebral Disability
  • 3–3: Severe Cerebral Disability
  • 4–4: Coma / Vegetative State
  • 5–5: Death / Brain Death

Important tips

  • Definitive assessment of neurological prognosis after cardiorespiratory arrest is usually performed after 72 hours, combining clinical examination, electroencephalogram, and other complementary exams.
  • Reassess the CPC category over time, as the neurological condition may evolve favorably or unfavorably in the following days/weeks.
  • Use the scale for standardized communication among the team and for documenting outcomes in institutional records.
  • Involve the family in the discussion about prognosis, with clear and welcoming language.

    Foundation

    The Cerebral Performance Category (CPC) was developed in the context of the Brain Resuscitation Clinical Trial (BRCT), classifying the functional neurological outcome of survivors of cardiorespiratory arrest into 5 categories, from CPC 1 (good cerebral performance) to CPC 5 (death/brain death).

    History

    Widely used in research and clinical practice on cardiopulmonary resuscitation and post-cardiac arrest care, being one of the most used standardized neurological outcomes in studies on therapeutic hypothermia and other post-resuscitation interventions.

    NANDA — Ineffective cerebral tissue perfusion

    Decreased cerebral blood flow, which may compromise health, related to the cardiac arrest event.

    NANDA — Dysfunctional family processes

    Family functioning that fails to support the well-being of its members, related to the impact of the patient's neurological outcome.

    NANDA — Grieving

    A normal and complex process that includes emotional, physical, spiritual, social, and intellectual responses and behaviors by which individuals, families, and communities incorporate an actual, anticipated, or perceived loss into their daily lives.

    NOC — Neurological status

    • Level of consciousness compatible with the identified category
    • Functional capacity documented and monitored
    • Family oriented about neurological prognosis

    NIC — Post-resuscitation care

    • Classify neurological outcome using the CPC scale throughout hospitalization and follow-up
    • Refer for neurological rehabilitation according to identified category
    • Support the family in understanding the neurological prognosis
    • Document the evolution of the CPC category over time

    Scientific Validation

    Validated and widely adopted as a standard neurological outcome in resuscitation studies, recommended by international cardiopulmonary resuscitation guidelines (ILCOR/AHA).

    Limitations

    It is a relatively simple scale, with only 5 broad categories, which may not capture specific nuances of neurological outcome. Definitive assessment of neurological prognosis after cardiorespiratory arrest should consider multiple complementary exams and the time of clinical evolution.

    How the Cerebral Performance Category (CPC) works

    The CPC classifies functional neurological outcome after cardiac arrest into 5 categories: CPC 1 (good cerebral performance), CPC 2 (moderate disability), CPC 3 (severe disability), CPC 4 (coma/vegetative state), and CPC 5 (death/brain death).

    Quick guide: CPC 1-2 = Good neurological outcome | CPC 3-5 = Poor neurological outcome.

    Can the CPC scale be used immediately after resuscitation?

    It can be used throughout hospitalization for monitoring, but definitive prognostic assessment is usually performed after at least 72 hours, combining clinical examination and complementary exams, according to resuscitation guidelines.

    Does the CPC replace the Glasgow Coma Scale?

    No. They are complementary tools: the Glasgow Coma Scale assesses the level of consciousness in the acute moment, while the CPC classifies the global functional neurological outcome, usually used in later assessments or as a study outcome.
