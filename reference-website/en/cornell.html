About this tool

Assess the presence and severity of depressive symptoms in patients with cognitive impairment/dementia, using 19 items grouped into 5 domains: mood signs, behavioral disturbance, physical signs, cyclic functions, and ideational disturbance. Indicated for depression screening in elderly patients with mild to severe dementia, especially when patient self-report is unreliable.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–10: No Significant Depression
  • 11–18: Probable Major Depressive Episode
  • 19–38: Definite Major Depressive Episode

Important tips

  • The CSDD is the most widely used depression scale specifically for patients with dementia, combining caregiver interview and brief patient interview.
  • Somatic items (physical complaints, appetite, weight, energy) may be confounded by physical illnesses — score 0 when the symptom is clearly due to a physical cause, not emotional.
  • The suicidal ideation item should be investigated with special care: a score of 1 indicates passive ideation (feels life is not worth living), a score of 2 indicates active ideation or recent attempt.
  • Reapply the scale periodically to monitor response to therapeutic interventions, especially antidepressant treatment.

    Foundation

    The CSDD assesses 19 items in 5 domains — mood signs (anxiety, sadness, lack of reactivity, irritability), behavioral disturbance (agitation, retardation, physical complaints, loss of interest), physical signs (appetite, weight, energy), cyclic functions (diurnal variation, sleep), and ideational disturbance (suicide, self-esteem, pessimism, delusions) — each scored from 0 to 2, totaling 0 to 38 points.

    History

    Developed by Alexopoulos, Abrams, Young, and Shamoian in 1988 at Cornell University, it was the first depression scale specifically validated for use in patients with dementia, combining caregiver information and direct patient observation.

    NANDA — Risk for suicide

    Susceptibility to self-harm with intent to die, identified especially by the suicidal ideation item of the scale.

    NANDA — Hopelessness

    Subjective state in which the individual sees limited or no personal alternatives and is unable to mobilize energy for their own benefit.

    NANDA — Disturbed sleep pattern

    Time-limited interruptions in the quantity and quality of sleep, related to items of cyclic functions on the scale.

    NANDA — Imbalanced nutrition: less than body requirements

    Insufficient nutrient intake to meet metabolic needs, related to loss of appetite and weight identified on the scale.

    NOC — Depression level

    • Systematically assessed CSDD score
    • Specialized referral performed as needed
    • Suicide risk assessed and monitored when ideation is present

    NIC — Mood management/suicide prevention

    • Administer the CSDD through a structured interview with the caregiver and a brief interview with the patient
    • Investigate and minimize interference of physical illnesses and disabilities in the scoring of somatic items
    • Carefully evaluate any identified suicidal ideation and notify the medical team immediately
    • Refer for psychiatric/geriatric evaluation according to obtained score
    • Reapply the scale periodically to monitor response to interventions (e.g., antidepressants)

    Scientific Validation

    Widely validated internationally for use in patients with mild to severe dementia, with good sensitivity and specificity. Scores above 10 suggest probable major depression, and above 18, definite major depression.

    Sensitivity: 93% · Specificity: 97%

    Limitations

    Somatic items (physical complaints, loss of appetite/weight, lack of energy) may be confounded by physical illnesses or disabilities unrelated to depression — it is recommended to score 0 on these items when the symptom is clearly attributable to a physical cause. Requires training for reliable application of the structured interview.

    How the Cornell Scale (CSDD) Works

    The CSDD sums the score (0 to 2) of 19 items distributed across 5 domains — mood signs, behavioral disturbance, physical signs, cyclic functions, and ideational disturbance — totaling 0 to 38 points.

    Quick guide: 0-10 = No significant depression | 11-18 = Probable major depression | 19-38 = Definite major depression.

    Can the CSDD be applied without the caregiver's collaboration?

    It is not recommended. The CSDD was designed as a structured interview that combines the caregiver's report (observing the patient's behavior over the previous week) with a brief direct interview with the patient, and this combination is essential for its validity in patients with cognitive impairment.

    Can physical illnesses artificially inflate the CSDD score?

    Yes. Elderly individuals often have physical illnesses with symptoms similar to depression (loss of appetite, weight, energy). To minimize falsely high scores, somatic items should be scored as 0 when clearly attributable to a physical condition, not a depressive disorder.
