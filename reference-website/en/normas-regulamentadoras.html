Regulatory Standards (NRs) - Occupational Safety and Health

Regulatory Standards (NRs)

Regulatory Standards (NRs) are rules from the Ministry of Labor that define obligations, rights, and duties of employers and workers to ensure safety, health, and adequate working conditions. They apply to different sectors and types of activity.

Year of Creation: 1977 and the first NRs were published on June 8, 1978

Entity: Ministry of Labor and Employment

How many NRs exist? 38, with NR-2 (Prior Inspection) and NR-27 (Professional Registration of Occupational Safety Technician) being revoked.

Revision: The elaboration and revision of regulatory standards are carried out using the tripartite parity system, recommended by the International Labour Organization (ILO), through groups and committees composed of representatives from government, employers, and workers.

NR-1 — General Provisions and Occupational Risk Management (GRO)

NR-1 is the "base" standard of the NRs: it defines general provisions, scope of application, terms and definitions common to other standards, and mainly establishes Occupational Risk Management (GRO). A central point is item 1.5, which defines minimum requirements for the Risk Management Program (PGR). In the same context, NR-1 reinforces the mandatory Accident Analysis (AAT) and Emergency Response Plan (PRE), forming the practical set of GRO (PGR + AAT + PRE).

NR-3 — Embargo and Interdiction

NR-3 deals with urgent embargo and interdiction measures applied when there is a serious and imminent risk to the worker. It uses two tables to classify the 'excess risk': one for individual exposure/low potential for victims and another for exposure with the possibility of affecting multiple victims simultaneously. The excess risk can be classified as E (Extreme), S (Substantial), M (Moderate), P (Small), or N (None). The regulation also defines criteria that guide the Labor Tax Auditor (AFT), who must apply embargo/interdiction in the smallest unit where the risk was identified.

NR-4 — SESMT (Specialized Services in Safety and Occupational Medicine)

NR-4 defines rules and criteria for sizing the SESMT, which is the technical structure responsible for supporting the company in occupational safety and health. Sizing depends on the establishment's risk grade and the number of workers, and can directly impact the need for professionals in the area. The regulation highlights the main SESMT categories: Safety Technician, Safety Engineer, Occupational Physician, Occupational Health Nurse, and Occupational Nursing Assistant, as well as the duties and responsibilities of these professionals.

NR-5 — CIPA (Internal Accident Prevention Committee)

NR-5 regulates the CIPA, a committee formed by workers that acts in the prevention of work-related accidents and diseases, strengthening the safety culture within the company. It defines rules for sizing, electoral process, composition, duties, and operation. The text also mentions Law No. 14,457/2022, which introduced measures for the prevention and combat of sexual harassment and other forms of violence within the scope of CIPA, expanding its role in worker protection.

NR-6 — Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

NR-6 establishes that companies must provide, free of charge, PPE appropriate to the risk, in perfect condition and functioning. PPE must be used when general or collective measures are not sufficient to eliminate the risk, while collective measures are being implemented, or in emergency situations. In other words, NR-6 organizes the employer's responsibility for supplying, instructing, and ensuring the correct use of PPE, guaranteeing individual protection when the risk cannot be fully controlled by other measures.

NR-7 — PCMSO (Occupational Health Medical Control Program)

NR-7 defines guidelines for the PCMSO, which focuses on preventing and early identifying health damage caused by work. Among the main guidelines are: tracking work-related injuries, detecting excessive exposures to harmful agents, and supporting the definition of employee fitness for specific functions. The regulation applies to organizations and also to public bodies with workers governed by the CLT. In practice, NR-7 organizes the 'line of care' for occupational medicine: monitoring, screening, and preventive actions.

NR-8 — Buildings

NR-8 establishes minimum technical requirements for safety and comfort in buildings used as work environments. It requires companies to adapt their physical structures (facilities, architectural conditions, and elements affecting safety) according to the minimum parameters of the regulation. This NR helps avoid structural risks and inadequate conditions, directly contributing to a safer and more functional work environment.

NR-9 — Assessment and Control of Occupational Exposures (physical, chemical, and biological agents)

NR-9 has been updated: previously associated with PPRA, it now focuses on the assessment and control of occupational exposures to physical, chemical, and biological agents. It connects to the PGR of NR-1, as agents must be evaluated when identified in risk management. In practice, NR-9 technically supports the identification, assessment, and control of these agents and directs preventive measures, reinforcing the transition from the old PPRA to an integrated logic with PGR and management.

NR-10 — Safety in Electrical Installations and Services

NR-10 establishes minimum requirements to ensure safety and health in activities involving electricity, protecting workers who interact directly or indirectly with electrical installations. The standard guides control measures and preventive systems to reduce the risk of electric shock, arc flash, burns, and other related events. It is essential in environments with electrical maintenance, building infrastructure, industry, and any activity with electrical exposure.

NR-11 — Transport, Movement, Storage, and Handling of Materials

NR-11 gathers safety guidelines and requirements for operations involving elevators, cranes, industrial conveyors, and transport machinery. It applies to companies that move and store loads, organizing obligations and precautions to prevent accidents such as load falls, crushing, collisions, and operational failures. It is a practical standard for logistics, warehouses, hospitals (internal movement), and industry.

NR-12 — Safety in Machinery and Equipment

NR-12 establishes minimum requirements to prevent work-related accidents and illnesses involving machinery and equipment, from design and manufacturing to installation, operation, maintenance, and commercialization. It is crucial to reduce risks such as exposed moving parts, protection failures, unintended activation, and lack of safety devices. It also opens space for OSH action in operator training and technical adjustments, as it requires protection and safe management of machine use.

NR-13 — Boilers, Pressure Vessels, Piping, and Metallic Tanks

NR-13 defines rules for managing the structural integrity and safety of boilers, pressure vessels, piping, and metallic tanks, covering installation, inspection, operation, and maintenance. The focus is to reduce risks of catastrophic failures, explosions, and leaks, protecting workers and property. It is highly technical and requires strict control of inspections and operational conditions.

NR-14 — Furnaces

NR-14 deals with safety in the operation of furnaces, considering construction, lining, heat, and installation. Its objective is to ensure that the equipment is operated safely, preventing accidents and risks from high temperatures and dangerous operational conditions. The standard applies to industrial activities and processes that use furnaces, reinforcing preventive measures aimed at thermal risk and equipment safety.

NR-15 — Unhealthy Activities and Operations

NR-15 defines unhealthy conditions when the worker is exposed to agents above the tolerance limits (by intensity, concentration, nature, or time). The objective is to alert and guide about situations that can cause health damage and require control, preventive measures, and, when applicable, legal classification (such as additional pay). It is an essential standard for assessing exposures and establishing limits and technical criteria.

NR-16 — Dangerous Activities and Operations

NR-16 addresses hazardous activities and defines safety measures for work with accentuated risk (for example, some activities with flammable materials, explosives, and other dangerous conditions). It also emphasizes that the characterization or decharacterization of hazardousness is the employer's responsibility, based on a technical report prepared by an Occupational Physician or Safety Engineer. This NR is linked to prevention and also to legal/labor effects when the activity is classified as hazardous.

NR-17 — Ergonomics

NR-17 establishes guidelines to adapt work to the psychophysiological characteristics of workers, promoting comfort, safety, health, and efficiency. It involves parameters for seated work, lifting and transporting materials, furniture, equipment, and environmental conditions of the workstation. In practice, it helps reduce musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, and decreased productivity, being widely applied in offices, administrative areas, healthcare, and industry.

NR-18 — Safety and Health in the Construction Industry

NR-18 is one of the most relevant for civil construction, as it establishes administrative, planning, and organization guidelines for implementing control measures and preventive systems in the processes, conditions, and work environment of construction. It organizes obligations and practices to reduce accidents on construction sites, activities at height, excavations, material handling, and other typical construction risks.

NR-19 — Explosives

NR-19 defines requirements and prevention measures for safety and health in all stages involving explosives: manufacturing, handling, storage, and transport. It also defines explosive as a material that undergoes rapid decomposition when initiated, with intense release of heat and pressure. This NR is critical for specific industrial sectors and requires high risk control due to the potential for serious accidents.

NR-20 — Flammables and Combustibles

NR-20 establishes minimum requirements for safety and health management in activities with flammable and combustible materials, including extraction, production, storage, transfer, handling, and manipulation. It addresses concepts such as flash point, fire point, and ignition point, important for classifying and controlling risk. The standard is essential for preventing fires, explosions, and leaks and requires robust preventive measures.

NR-21 — Open Air Work

NR-21 deals with work performed in the open air and establishes protection requirements, such as shelter against weather, as well as standards for work in swampy/flooded areas to avoid risks of diseases and endemics. This NR is important for rural activities, outdoor works, and services on public roads, as it considers exposure to climate and natural unhealthy environments.

NR-22 — Occupational Safety and Health in Mining

NR-22 is aimed at underground and open-pit mining, artisanal mining, ore processing, and mineral research. It defines technical and organizational care so that the planning and execution of mineral activity occur with a permanent focus on safety and health. It addresses characteristic risks such as collapses, dust, explosives, heavy machinery, and extreme environmental conditions.

NR-23 — Fire Protection

NR-23 defines fire prevention measures in work environments, which must be adopted according to state legislation and applicable technical standards. Even though it is a short standard, it gathers essential requirements: safety guidelines that all workers must know and mandatory prevention and response mechanisms. It serves as a basis for organizing escape routes, signage, and emergency preparedness.

NR-24 — Sanitary and Comfort Conditions in Workplaces

NR-24 defines minimum hygiene and comfort standards, such as bathrooms, washbasins, and sizing according to the shift with the largest number of workers. The standard aims to ensure dignified and adequate conditions for workers, avoiding sanitary risks, discomfort, and health problems resulting from insufficient or inadequate structure.

NR-25 — Industrial Waste

NR-25 establishes safety and health requirements for the management of industrial waste (solid, liquid, and gaseous) that is not similar to domestic waste. It includes examples such as ashes, sludge, oils, dust, dregs, leached substances, and waste generated by pollution control equipment, effluents, and atmospheric emissions. In practice, it guides how to handle waste to reduce chemical/biological risks and impacts on health and the environment.

NR-26 — Safety Signaling

NR-26 defines rules for safety signage on hazardous items, including the use of colors, classification, preventive labeling, and a safety data sheet. The objective is to ensure clear communication of risks, reducing accidents due to lack of knowledge, handling errors, or lack of information about hazardous substances and areas.

NR-28 — Inspection and Penalties

NR-28 establishes inspection procedures and applicable penalties when occupational safety and health standards are violated. It functions as a "disciplinary" standard, defining how inspection activities occur and what legal consequences may arise, reinforcing the importance of effective compliance with NRs.

NR-29 — Safety and Health in Port Work

NR-29 establishes prevention measures and guidelines for managing occupational risks in port work. It aims to protect against accidents and diseases, facilitate first aid, and ensure better safety and health conditions for port workers, both in onboard and onshore operations. It also applies to workers in organized ports and private-use port and retroport facilities.

NR-30 — Safety and Health in Waterway Work

NR-30 regulates requirements for the protection of safety and health in waterway work and the prevention of injuries and illnesses. It applies to work on commercial vessels of national and foreign flags (when applicable by international conventions), including vessels used for service provision. It is essential for organizing procedures, working conditions, and prevention of typical risks in the waterway environment.

NR-31 — Safety and Health in Rural Work

NR-31 applies to activities in agriculture, livestock, forestry, forest exploitation, and aquaculture, considering different forms of employment relationships and the location of activities. It also applies to industrial exploitation within rural establishments. This NR covers risks such as rural machinery, pesticides, heat, physical exertion, and environmental conditions, structuring preventive measures in rural settings.

NR-32 — Safety and Health in Healthcare Services

NR-32 defines guidelines to protect workers in health services and also those who work in health promotion and assistance in general. It considers health services any building intended for public care and actions of promotion, recovery, assistance, research, and teaching at any level of complexity. In practice, it is essential for controlling biological, chemical, sharps, health waste risks, and routines that expose workers to harm.

NR-33 — Safety and Health in Confined Spaces

NR-33 defines requirements for characterizing confined spaces, risk management, and prevention measures to protect workers who interact directly or indirectly in these environments. A confined space is one not designed for continuous human occupancy, has limited entry/exit, and may have a hazardous atmosphere. NR-33 is critical because these environments can involve risks of asphyxiation, poisoning, explosion, and death, requiring strict controls.

NR-34 — Construction, Repair, and Shipbreaking Industry

NR-34 establishes minimum requirements and protection measures for safety, health, and the work environment in the naval industry (construction, repair, and dismantling). It applies to both facilities and vessels/structures (ships, boats, launches, fixed/floating platforms). It addresses typical risks such as welding, confined spaces, work at height, material handling, and exposure to chemical agents.

NR-35 — Work at Height

NR-35 defines requirements and prevention measures for work at height, including planning, organization, and execution, ensuring safety and health of those involved. It applies to activities with a height difference above 2 meters where there is a risk of falling. In practice, it requires strict process control, as falls are one of the leading causes of serious workplace accidents.

NR-36 — Slaughter and Processing of Meat and Derivatives

NR-36 establishes minimum requirements for assessment, control, and monitoring of risks in the slaughter and meat processing industry and derivatives, ensuring safety, health, and quality of work life. It targets a sector with high ergonomic risk, repetitiveness, cold, cuts, and intense pace, requiring specific preventive measures without replacing compliance with other NRs.

NR-37 — Safety and Health on Oil Platforms

NR-37 establishes safety, health, and living conditions requirements for work on board oil platforms in Brazilian Jurisdictional Waters. It applies to national and foreign platforms and authorized Maintenance and Safety Units. It is essential because the offshore environment involves high risks (fire, explosion, confinement, evacuation, weather conditions, and isolation).

NR-38 — Urban Cleaning and Solid Waste Management

NR-38 defines requirements and prevention measures to ensure safety and health conditions in urban cleaning and solid waste management activities. It targets a sector with frequent exposure to biological risks, sharps, traffic, machinery, physical exertion, and environmental conditions, organizing measures to reduce accidents and illnesses.
