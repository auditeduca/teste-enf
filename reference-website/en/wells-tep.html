About this tool

Estimate the pre-test clinical probability of pulmonary embolism (PE), guiding the diagnostic strategy (D-dimer versus direct CT pulmonary angiography). Indicated for patients with clinical suspicion of pulmonary embolism (sudden dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, unexplained tachycardia).

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–1.9: Low Probability
  • 2–6: Moderate Probability
  • 6.1–12.5: High Probability

Important tips

  • The items 'clinical signs of DVT' and 'alternative diagnosis less likely' have the highest weight (3 points each) in the score.
  • In high clinical probability patients, do not use D-dimer to rule out PE — prioritize CT pulmonary angiography directly.
  • Always combine with signs of hemodynamic instability, which may indicate massive PE and require immediate management.
  • Reassess the patient clinically if symptoms persist despite initial negative workup.

    Foundation

    The Wells Score for PE was developed by Philip Wells and colleagues to stratify the pre-test clinical probability of pulmonary embolism, combining clinical signs, risk factors, and the presence of an alternative diagnosis.

    History

    Widely used in emergency departments to guide the diagnostic strategy, avoiding unnecessary imaging in low-probability patients with negative D-dimer.

    NANDA — Impaired gas exchange

    Excess or deficit in oxygenation and/or carbon dioxide elimination at the alveolar-capillary membrane.

    NANDA — Ineffective breathing pattern

    Inspiration and/or expiration that does not provide adequate ventilation.

    NANDA — Decreased cardiac output

    Inadequate amount of blood pumped by the heart to meet the body's metabolic demands, associated with right ventricular overload in massive PE.

    NOC — Respiratory status: gas exchange

    • Adequate oxygen saturation
    • Absence of dyspnea at rest
    • Respiratory rate within expected limits

    NIC — Respiratory care

    • Apply the Wells Score for PE systematically
    • Monitor oxygen saturation and respiratory rate continuously
    • Assist in obtaining D-dimer and preparation for CT angiography as indicated
    • Immediately communicate signs of hemodynamic instability to the medical team

    Scientific Validation

    Extensively validated in the literature, with good risk stratification capacity when combined with D-dimer, being one of the most used scores worldwide for this purpose, alongside the revised Geneva score.

    Limitations

    In high clinical probability patients, D-dimer should not be used as a screening test to exclude PE — imaging (CT angiography) should be prioritized directly.

    How the Wells Score for PE works

    The Wells Score for PE sums weighted points from 7 clinical criteria — clinical signs of DVT, alternative diagnosis, heart rate, immobilization/recent surgery, previous DVT/PE, hemoptysis, and malignancy — totaling up to 12.5 points.

    Quick guide: less than 2 = Low probability | 2-6 = Moderate probability | more than 6 = High probability.

    Does the Wells Score for PE confirm the diagnosis alone?

    No. It is a pre-test probability stratification tool, which must be combined with D-dimer and/or CT pulmonary angiography for diagnostic confirmation or exclusion.

    Why not use D-dimer in high-probability patients?

    D-dimer has high sensitivity but low specificity. In high clinical probability patients, even a negative D-dimer does not safely rule out PE, making direct imaging necessary.
