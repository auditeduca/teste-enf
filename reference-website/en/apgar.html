About this tool

Rapidly assess the newborn's vitality at 1 and 5 minutes of life, guiding the need for resuscitation maneuvers. Systematically applied to every newborn at 1 and 5 minutes after birth; repeated every 5 minutes if the score remains below 7.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–3: Severely Depressed
  • 4–6: Moderately Depressed
  • 7–10: Normal / Good Vitality

Important tips

  • Always assess at 1 and 5 minutes of life; repeat every 5 minutes if the score is less than 7.
  • Do not delay resuscitation maneuvers to calculate the score — resuscitation takes priority.
  • Document the 1-minute and 5-minute scores separately.
  • Remember the mnemonic A-P-G-A-R: Appearance (color), Pulse (HR), Grimace (reflex irritability), Activity (muscle tone), Respiration.

Action Plan

  • Assess HR, respiration, tone, irritability, and color.
  • Record 1-minute and 5-minute scores separately.
  • Initiate resuscitation if Apgar < 7 — do not wait for the calculation.
  • Repeat every 5 minutes if score remains < 7.

Assessment & Monitoring (NOC)

Monitor newborn vitality: heart rate, respiratory effort, muscle tone, reflex irritability, and skin color. Reassess per institutional protocol.

    Foundation

    The Apgar Score was developed by anesthesiologist Virginia Apgar in 1952 as a rapid and standardized method to assess the clinical condition of the newborn immediately after birth, allowing early identification of the need for resuscitation.

    History

    Adopted worldwide as the standard for immediate neonatal assessment, recommended by the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) and the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) as part of the birth routine.

    NANDA — Ineffective breathing pattern

    Inspiration and/or expiration that does not provide adequate ventilation.

    NANDA — Risk for unstable blood glucose level

    Vulnerability to variation in blood glucose/sugar levels, which may compromise newborn health.

    NOC — Respiratory status: newborn

    • Adequate respiratory rate
    • Absence of cyanosis
    • Vigorous cry

    NOC — Newborn adaptation to extrauterine life

    • Thermal stability
    • Cardiorespiratory stability
    • Reflexes present

    NIC — Newborn care: immediate

    • Dry and warm the newborn
    • Assess Apgar at 1 and 5 minutes
    • Suction airways if necessary
    • Perform skin-to-skin contact

    Scientific Validation

    Widely validated in the literature as an indicator of neonatal vitality, although it should not be used alone to predict long-term neurological prognosis.

    Limitations

    The Apgar score alone should not be used to diagnose perinatal asphyxia or predict long-term neurological outcomes — it must be interpreted together with cord blood gas analysis and a complete clinical examination.

    How the Apgar Score Works

    The Apgar score assesses 5 signs in the newborn — heart rate, respiratory effort, muscle tone, reflex irritability, and skin color — each scored from 0 to 2. The sum ranges from 0 to 10 and is measured at 1 and 5 minutes of life.

    Quick guide: 7-10 = Normal | 4-6 = Moderately depressed | 0-3 = Severely depressed (immediate resuscitation).

    What to do if the 5-minute Apgar is still low?

    Continue reassessing every 5 minutes up to 20 minutes of life, maintaining indicated resuscitation maneuvers and documenting each score.

    Does the Apgar replace umbilical cord blood gas analysis?

    No. They are complementary assessments. Cord blood gas provides objective information about acid-base balance, while the Apgar is a rapid clinical assessment of vitality.
