About this tool

Assess functional balance through 14 standardized mobility tasks, estimating fall risk in older adults and other populations. Indicated for balance assessment in older adults, post-stroke patients, Parkinson's disease, or other conditions affecting balance.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–20: High Fall Risk
  • 21–40: Moderate Fall Risk
  • 41–56: Low Fall Risk

Important tips

  • The Berg Scale assesses 14 functional tasks, each scored from 0 (unable) to 4 (independent and safe), totaling 56 points.
  • Different cutoff points are used in the literature for fall risk (40 to 45 points) — use the value adopted by your institution's protocol.
  • Adequate space, a chair, a stool, and an object to pick up from the floor are required for full administration.
  • Combine results with history of previous falls and other tools for a more comprehensive risk assessment.

    Berg Balance Scale: 56 points — Low Risk of Falls

    Foundation

    The Berg Balance Scale assesses 14 functional tasks of static and dynamic balance, each scored from 0 to 4, totaling a score from 0 to 56 points, to evaluate functional balance and fall risk.

    History

    Developed by Katherine Berg and colleagues, it is one of the most widely used tools worldwide for balance assessment, extensively employed in geriatrics, neurology, and rehabilitation.

    NANDA — Risk for falls

    Increased susceptibility to falls, which may cause physical harm and compromise health, related to impaired functional balance.

    NANDA — Impaired physical mobility

    Limitation in independent, intentional physical movement of the body or one or more extremities.

    NANDA — Risk for disuse syndrome

    Risk for deterioration of body systems as a consequence of prescribed or unavoidable musculoskeletal inactivity, related to limitations in functional balance.

    NOC — Balance

    • Berg Balance Scale score assessed and monitored over time
    • Participation in balance exercise program when indicated
    • Absence of falls during follow-up

    NIC — Fall prevention

    • Apply the Berg Balance Scale in functional assessment
    • Refer to physical therapy according to identified degree of impairment
    • Adapt the environment to reduce fall risks
    • Educate on proper use of gait assistive devices
    • Reassess functional balance progression periodically

    Scientific Validation

    Extensively validated in diverse populations, including older adults, post-stroke patients, and those with Parkinson's disease, with good interrater reliability and correlation with other measures of mobility and fall risk.

    Limitations

    Requires considerable administration time (15-20 minutes) and adequate space. The optimal cutoff for fall prediction varies across studies and populations (values cited between 40 and 45 points).

    How does the Berg Balance Scale work?

    The Berg Balance Scale assesses 14 functional balance tasks (sitting, standing, transferring, turning, reaching, among others), each scored from 0 to 4, summing to a total score from 0 to 56 points.

    Quick guide: 0-20 = High fall risk | 21-40 = Moderate risk | 41-56 = Low risk.

    What cutoff point is used for fall risk on the Berg Balance Scale?

    The literature cites different cutoff points, generally between 40 and 45 points. This calculator uses a three-range stratification (0-20 high risk, 21-40 moderate risk, 41-56 low risk), but the institutional protocol may adopt a specific cutoff.

    How long does it take to administer the Berg Balance Scale?

    Full administration of the 14 tasks usually takes 15 to 20 minutes and requires adequate space, a chair with and without armrests, a stool, and an object to be picked up from the floor.
