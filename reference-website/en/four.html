About this tool

To assess in detail the level of consciousness in critically ill patients, including brainstem reflexes and respiratory pattern, overcoming limitations of the Glasgow Coma Scale in intubated patients. Indicated for neurocritical patients, including those on mechanical ventilation or intubated, where the Glasgow Coma Scale has its verbal component compromised.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–8: Severe Impairment
  • 9–12: Moderate Impairment
  • 13–16: Mild Impairment / Preserved

Important tips

  • The FOUR Scale is especially useful in intubated patients, where the Glasgow Coma Scale loses verbal assessment.
  • Assess brainstem reflexes carefully and with adequate training, as they are the most specific component of the scale.
  • Low scores on the motor component (generalized myoclonus) have a strong association with poor prognosis.
  • Reassess serially to monitor the evolution of the neurological condition.

    Foundation

    The FOUR (Full Outline of UnResponsiveness) Coma Scale was developed by Wijdicks and colleagues at the Mayo Clinic in 2005, assessing 4 components (ocular response, motor response, brainstem reflexes, and respiratory pattern), each scored from 0 to 4, totaling 0 to 16 points.

    History

    Developed to overcome limitations of the Glasgow Coma Scale in intubated patients (inability to assess verbal response) and to detect minimally conscious states and locked-in syndrome through assessment of brainstem reflexes.

    NANDA — Ineffective cerebral tissue perfusion

    Decreased cerebral blood circulation, potentially compromising health.

    NANDA — Ineffective breathing pattern

    Inspiration and/or expiration that does not provide adequate ventilation.

    NANDA — Risk for aspiration

    Risk of entry of gastrointestinal secretions, oropharyngeal secretions, or solids/liquids into the tracheobronchial passages, associated with diminished protective reflexes.

    NOC — Neurological status

    • Level of consciousness appropriate to clinical condition
    • Preserved brainstem reflexes
    • Adequate respiratory pattern

    NIC — Neurologic Monitoring

    • Assess ocular response, motor response, brainstem reflexes, and respiratory pattern
    • Document the FOUR score at each assessment
    • Immediately report any deterioration to the medical team
    • Assess airway protective reflexes (cough, corneal)

    Scientific Validation

    Comparative studies demonstrate good interrater reliability and predictive ability for mortality comparable or superior to the Glasgow Coma Scale, with the advantage of being applicable in intubated patients.

    Limitations

    It is a more complex scale than the Glasgow Coma Scale, requiring greater team training for correct application of brainstem reflex components.

    How does the FOUR Coma Scale work?

    The FOUR Score assesses 4 components — eye response, motor response, brainstem reflexes, and respiratory pattern — each scored from 0 to 4, totaling 0 to 16 points. It was developed to overcome the limitation of the Glasgow Coma Scale in intubated patients.

    Quick guide: 13-16 = Preserved/mild | 9-12 = Moderate | 0-8 = Severe.

    Does the FOUR Scale replace the Glasgow Coma Scale?

    Not necessarily. Both are used in clinical practice, but the FOUR is particularly useful in intubated patients, where the Glasgow verbal assessment cannot be performed.

    Is special training required to apply the FOUR Scale?

    Yes. Assessment of brainstem reflexes requires specific team training, being more complex than applying the Glasgow Coma Scale.
