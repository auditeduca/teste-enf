About this tool

Screen for mild cognitive impairment (MCI) and dementia by assessing 7 cognitive domains: visuospatial/executive, naming, attention, language, abstraction, delayed memory, and orientation. Indicated for screening of mild cognitive impairment, early Alzheimer's disease, and other dementias, being more sensitive than the MMSE for mild deficits.

Interpretation ranges

  • 26–31: Normal Cognition
  • 18–25: Mild Cognitive Impairment
  • 10–17: Moderate Cognitive Impairment
  • 0–9: Severe Cognitive Impairment

Important tips

  • MoCA is more sensitive than MMSE for detecting mild cognitive impairment (MCI) because it includes more complex executive tasks.
  • Do not forget the +1 point adjustment for patients with 12 years or less of formal education — this adjustment is already incorporated as a separate item in this calculator.
  • The classic cutoff is 26/30: scores equal to or above suggest normal cognition.
  • Administer in a quiet environment without rushing, and consider factors such as fatigue, anxiety, and sensory deficits that may reduce performance independently of cognition.

    Foundation

    MoCA assesses 7 cognitive domains — visuospatial/executive (5 pts), naming (3 pts), attention (6 pts), language (3 pts), abstraction (2 pts), delayed recall (5 pts), and orientation (6 pts) — totaling 30 points, with 1 point added for patients with 12 years or less of formal education.

    History

    Developed by Ziad Nasreddine in 1996 and published in 2005, MoCA was created to address the low sensitivity of the MMSE in detecting mild cognitive impairment, incorporating more challenging executive tasks.

    NANDA — Impaired memory

    Inability to remember or recall fragments of information or behavioral skills, identified by low performance in memory and delayed recall domains of MoCA.

    NANDA — Chronic confusion

    Irreversible, long-standing and/or progressive deterioration of intellect and personality, characterized by decreased ability to interpret environmental stimuli and thought processes.

    NANDA — Risk for falls

    Increased susceptibility to falls related to cognitive and judgment impairment identified on assessment.

    NOC — Cognition

    • MoCA score assessed and documented
    • Referral made according to identified need
    • Reversible factors investigated and treated when present

    NIC — Cognitive stimulation and monitoring

    • Administer MoCA in a calm, distraction-free environment, respecting the patient's time
    • Consider education adjustment (+1 point if ≤ 12 years of formal education)
    • Refer for neuropsychological or neurological evaluation according to result
    • Investigate reversible causes of cognitive decline (delirium, depression, medication use, metabolic abnormalities)
    • Educate family/caregivers on support strategies and patient safety

    Scientific Validation

    Widely validated internationally and in Brazil, with superior sensitivity to MMSE for detecting MCI. The classic cutoff of 26 has high sensitivity but lower specificity, especially in populations with low education — caution is recommended, and use of Brazilian population-adjusted norms when available.

    Sensitivity: 90% · Specificity: 87%

    Limitations

    Education and sociocultural level strongly influence performance, even with the +1 point adjustment. It should be interpreted together with a complete clinical assessment, not in isolation for dementia diagnosis.

    How the MoCA (Montreal Cognitive Assessment) works

    The MoCA sums the scores of 7 cognitive domains — visuospatial/executive, naming, attention, language, abstraction, delayed recall, and orientation — totaling up to 30 points, with a possible +1 point adjustment for low education.

    Quick guide: ≥26 = Normal | 18-25 = Mild MCI | 10-17 = Moderate | <10 = Severe.

    What is the difference between the MoCA and the MEEM/MMSE?

    Both are cognitive screening tests, but the MoCA includes more complex executive and attention tasks (trail making, verbal fluency, abstraction), making it more sensitive for detecting mild cognitive impairment (MCI), while the MMSE tends to perform better in screening for established dementia.

    Is the MoCA alone sufficient to diagnose dementia or MCI?

    No. The MoCA is a screening tool and should be interpreted together with clinical history, complete neurological examination, and, when indicated, formal neuropsychological assessment and complementary exams.
