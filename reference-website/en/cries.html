About this tool

Assess postoperative pain in newborns using 5 indicators: crying, need for supplemental oxygen, increased vital signs, facial expression, and sleeplessness/alertness. Indicated for postoperative pain assessment in newborns with gestational age ≥ 32 weeks admitted to the neonatal ICU.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–3: No Pain or Minimal Pain
  • 4–5: Pain Present - Reassessment Needed
  • 6–10: Significant Pain - Analgesia Indicated

Important tips

  • The name CRIES is an acronym: Crying, Requires O2, Increased vital signs, Expression, and Sleeplessness.
  • It is essential to compare heart rate and blood pressure with the newborn's own preoperative baseline values, obtained during a stress-free period.
  • Measure blood pressure last, as the procedure may awaken the newborn and hinder assessment of other items.
  • Consider other causes of oxygenation changes (atelectasis, pneumothorax, sedation) before attributing the drop in saturation solely to pain.

    Foundation

    CRIES assesses 5 indicators — crying, need for supplemental oxygen, increased vital signs, facial expression, and sleeplessness — each scored from 0 to 2, totaling a score from 0 to 10 points, specific for postoperative pain in newborns.

    History

    Developed by Krechel and Bildner in 1995, CRIES was one of the first scales specifically constructed for postoperative pain assessment in neonates, combining objective physiological indicators with behavioral indicators.

    NANDA — Acute pain

    Unpleasant sensory and emotional experience associated with actual tissue damage (postoperative), identified by physiological and behavioral indicators in the newborn.

    NOC — Pain control

    • CRIES score systematically assessed in the postoperative period
    • Uses pharmacologic and non-pharmacologic relief measures as indicated
    • Reduction in CRIES score after analgesic intervention

    NIC — Neonatal postoperative pain management

    • Apply CRIES regularly in the postoperative period per institutional protocol
    • Compare vital signs with the newborn's own preoperative baseline values
    • Use non-pharmacologic comfort measures (non-nutritive sucking, oral glucose, facilitated tucking)
    • Administer analgesia per prescription for high scores (≥6)
    • Record CRIES score and response to intervention in the medical record

    Scientific Validation

    Validated for newborns with gestational age of 32 weeks or more in neonatal intensive care units, with good reliability and validity in detecting postoperative pain.

    Limitations

    Requires access to preoperative baseline parameters (HR, BP, saturation) of the newborn for adequate comparison. Other causes of changes in vital signs and oxygenation (atelectasis, pneumothorax, excessive sedation) should be considered before attributing the change solely to pain.

    How does the CRIES Scale work?

    CRIES sums the score (0 to 2) of 5 indicators — crying, need for O2, increased vital signs, facial expression, and sleeplessness — totaling 0 to 10 points.

    Quick guide: 0-3 = No/minimal pain | 4-5 = Pain present, reassess | 6-10 = Significant pain, analgesia indicated.

    Can CRIES be used in any newborn?

    CRIES was validated for newborns with gestational age of 32 weeks or more, being specific for the context of postoperative pain in the neonatal ICU.

    Does a drop in oxygen saturation always indicate pain?

    Not necessarily. Other causes such as atelectasis, pneumothorax, or excessive sedation can also cause drops in oxygenation and should be considered in clinical assessment before attributing the change solely to pain.
