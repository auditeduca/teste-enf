About this tool

Objectively assess fall risk in hospitalized patients, based on 7 categories: age, fall history, elimination, medications, care equipment, mobility, and cognition. Indicated for fall risk assessment in adult hospitalized patients, at admission and periodic reassessment per institutional protocol.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–5: Low Fall Risk
  • 6–13: Moderate Fall Risk
  • 14–35: High Fall Risk

Important tips

  • Before calculating the numeric score, check automatic classification criteria: more than one fall in 6 months, fall during current hospitalization, or special precautions (e.g., seizure) automatically classify the patient as High Risk.
  • Complete paralysis or total immobilization automatically classify the patient as Low Risk, without needing to calculate the numeric score.
  • Mobility and cognition items are multiple selection — all present factors should be summed, not just the highest scoring one.
  • The item 'sedation in the last 24 hours' in the medications category is worth 7 points, the highest individual weight among all scale items.

    Foundation

    JHFRAT sums points from 7 categories — age, fall history, elimination, medications, care equipment, mobility (3 items), and cognition (3 items) — totaling a score that can range from 0 to 35 points in this calculator (excluding automatic high/low-risk classification criteria).

    History

    Developed by the Johns Hopkins Health System Corporation in 2007, it is widely used in hospitals in the United States and other countries as a standardized fall risk assessment tool for hospitalized patients.

    NANDA — Risk for falls

    Increased susceptibility to falls, which may cause physical harm and compromise health, identified by risk factors assessed in JHFRAT.

    NANDA — Impaired physical mobility

    Limitation in independent physical movement, related to mobility deficits identified on the scale.

    NANDA — Acute/Chronic Confusion

    Sudden onset or progressive deterioration of reversible disturbances in consciousness, attention, cognition, and perception, related to cognition items on the scale.

    NOC — Fall prevention behavior

    • JHFRAT score assessed at admission and reassessed periodically
    • Prevention measures implemented according to identified risk category
    • Absence of falls during hospitalization

    NIC — Fall prevention

    • Apply JHFRAT on admission and reassess per institutional protocol and changes in clinical status
    • Check for automatic high-risk conditions (more than one fall in 6 months, fall during hospitalization, special precautions) before applying the numeric score
    • Implement prevention measures proportional to risk category (low, moderate, high)
    • Review high-risk fall medications with medical team/pharmacy
    • Educate patient and family on use of appropriate footwear, call bell, and requesting assistance to ambulate

    Scientific Validation

    Validated in comparative studies with other fall risk scales (such as the Morse Scale), with adequate sensitivity and specificity for risk screening in the hospital setting.

    Sensitivity: 68% · Specificity: 80%

    Limitations

    Before calculating the numeric score, check automatic classification criteria: history of more than one fall in 6 months, fall during current hospitalization, or patient considered high risk per protocol (e.g., seizure precautions) automatically classify the patient as high risk, regardless of score. Complete paralysis or total immobilization classify as low risk. This numeric calculator does not replace that initial screening.

    How the Johns Hopkins Scale (JHFRAT) works

    JHFRAT sums points from 7 categories — age, fall history, elimination, medications, care equipment, mobility, and cognition — totaling up to 35 points in this calculator.

    Quick guide: 0-5 = Low risk | 6-13 = Moderate risk | >13 = High risk. Automatic classification criteria (history of multiple falls, fall during hospitalization, complete immobilization) take precedence over the numeric score.

    Does the numeric JHFRAT replace the automatic high/low risk initial screening?

    No. Before calculating the numeric score, always check the automatic classification criteria (history of more than one fall in 6 months, fall during current hospitalization, special precautions, or complete paralysis/immobilization). These criteria take precedence over the numeric calculation presented in this calculator.

    What is the difference between JHFRAT and the Morse Scale?

    Both assess fall risk in hospitalized patients, but use different categories and weights. Comparative studies show similar psychometric performance between the two scales, and the choice usually depends on the adopted institutional protocol.

    This tool is for educational and clinical decision support purposes. Results should always be reviewed and validated by a qualified nursing professional, according to patient assessment and institutional protocols.
