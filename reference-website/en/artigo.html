The Apgar Score: History and Evolution

How Virginia Apgar revolutionized neonatal assessment in 1953 and the evolution of the score to the present day, with implications for nursing practice.

8 min read 4.9 · 320 reviews Updated July 2026

History

The Apgar Score was developed by anesthesiologist Virginia Apgar in 1952 as a rapid and standardized method to assess the clinical condition of the newborn immediately after birth, allowing early identification of the need for resuscitation.

"Apgar published her method in 1953 in Current Researches in Anesthesia and Analgesia, proposing a systematic assessment at 1 and 5 minutes of life."

The 5 Parameters

The score evaluates 5 newborn signs, each scored from 0 to 2, totaling 0 to 10:

  • Appearance (skin color): 0 = cyanotic/pale, 1 = pink body with cyanotic extremities, 2 = completely pink
  • Pulse (frequência cardíaca): 0 = ausente, 1 = <100 bpm, 2 = ≥100 bpm
  • Grimace (reflex irritability): 0 = no response, 1 = grimace, 2 = cry/cough/sneeze
  • Activity (muscle tone): 0 = flaccid, 1 = some flexion, 2 = active movement
  • Respiration (respiratory effort): 0 = absent, 1 = weak/irregular, 2 = good/strong cry
Mnemonic A-P-G-A-R: Appearance, Pulse, Grimace, Activity, Respiration — the initials form the creator's name.

Clinical Interpretation

7 to 10 — Normal / Good Vitality

Newborn with good vitality at birth. Management: routine care, skin-to-skin contact, timely cord clamping, routine monitoring.

4 to 6 — Moderately Depressed

May require stimulation, supplemental oxygen, or assisted ventilation. Management: tactile stimulation, supplemental O₂ if indicated, continuous monitoring, reassess at 5 minutes.

0 to 3 — Severely Depressed

Severely depressed newborn. Requires immediate neonatal resuscitation maneuvers per institutional protocol.

Critical Points for Nursing

  1. Never delay resuscitation to calculate the score — resuscitation has absolute priority.
  2. Document separately the 1-minute and 5-minute scores.
  3. Reassess every 5 minutes if the score remains below 7, up to 20 minutes.
  4. Apgar does not replace cord blood gas analysis — they are complementary assessments.
  5. Do not use alone to predict long-term neurological prognosis.
