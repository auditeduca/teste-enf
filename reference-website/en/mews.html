About this tool

Early detection of signs of clinical deterioration in hospitalized patients, based on simple physiological parameter scoring, guiding the need for intervention and escalation of care. Indicated for serial monitoring of inpatients in wards, allowing early identification of patients at risk of deterioration and expediting activation of the rapid response team.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–2: Low Risk
  • 3–4: Moderate Risk
  • 5–14: High Risk

Important tips

  • Record all 5 parameters at each assessment to avoid underestimating the total score.
  • A single highly altered parameter already justifies communication to the team, even if the total score appears low.
  • Increase the frequency of reassessment proportionally to the obtained score.
  • Use MEWS as a complement, never a substitute, for the team's clinical judgment.

    Foundation

    The MEWS (Modified Early Warning Score) is a physiological scoring system developed from the original EWS, used to early identify patients at risk of clinical deterioration in inpatient units, by summing points assigned to 5 parameters: respiratory rate, heart rate, systolic blood pressure, temperature, and level of consciousness.

    History

    Widely adopted in UK hospitals and later incorporated into various patient safety protocols and rapid response teams worldwide, being a precursor to the more current NEWS/NEWS2.

    NANDA — Risk for Ineffective Tissue Perfusion

    Risk for decrease in blood circulation to tissue, which may compromise health.

    NANDA — Ineffective breathing pattern

    Inspiration and/or expiration that does not provide adequate ventilation.

    NANDA — Risk for Acute Confusion

    Risk for abrupt onset of reversible disturbances of consciousness, attention, cognition, and perception.

    NOC — Vital signs

    • Respiratory rate within expected limits
    • Heart rate within expected limits
    • Blood pressure within expected limits
    • Preserved level of consciousness

    NIC — Vital Signs Monitoring

    • Measure and record respiratory rate, heart rate, blood pressure, and temperature
    • Assess level of consciousness using AVPU scale
    • Calculate and document MEWS score at each assessment
    • Communicate elevated scores immediately to medical team
    • Activate rapid response protocol when indicated

    Scientific Validation

    Studies demonstrate an association between high MEWS scores and increased risk of ICU admission, cardiorespiratory arrest, and in-hospital mortality, being a useful tool for triage and care prioritization.

    Limitations

    Early warning scores like MEWS do not replace clinical judgment. They may show variations in sensitivity depending on the studied population; more recent versions like NEWS2 have been preferred in many institutions for greater predictive accuracy.

    How MEWS works

    MEWS sums points from 0 to 3 assigned to 5 physiological parameters — respiratory rate, heart rate, systolic blood pressure, temperature, and level of consciousness (AVPU) — to generate a total score that indicates the risk of clinical deterioration in hospitalized patients.

    Quick guide: 0-2 = Low risk | 3-4 = Moderate risk | ≥5 = High risk, trigger urgent evaluation.

    How often should MEWS be calculated?

    Frequency varies according to institutional protocol and the obtained score: patients with low scores may be reassessed per unit routine, while higher scores require more frequent reassessment, often hourly.

    What is the difference between MEWS and NEWS2?

    NEWS2 is a more recent and refined version of the early warning score, developed by the Royal College of Physicians of the UK, incorporating additional parameters such as oxygen saturation and use of supplemental oxygen therapy, with greater predictive accuracy than the original MEWS.
