About this tool

Estimate the probability of acute appendicitis from the combination of clinical signs and symptoms with simple laboratory tests, aiding in triage and decision-making regarding further investigation or surgical evaluation. Indicated for patients with abdominal pain suggestive of acute appendicitis in the emergency department or emergency unit.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–4: Low Probability
  • 5–6: Possible Appendicitis
  • 7–8: Probable Appendicitis
  • 9–10: Very Probable Appendicitis

Important tips

  • The mnemonic MANTRELS helps remember the 8 criteria: Migration, Anorexia, Nausea/vomiting, Tenderness (RLQ pain), Rebound, Elevated temperature, Leukocytosis, Shift to left.
  • Scores of 7 or more have higher sensitivity for acute appendicitis and usually prompt surgical evaluation.
  • In special populations (children, elderly, pregnant women), always consider complementing with imaging, regardless of the score.
  • Reassess the score if the clinical picture evolves, as the presentation of appendicitis may change over time.

    Foundation

    The Alvarado Score was developed by Alfredo Alvarado in 1986, combining 3 symptoms (migration of pain, anorexia, nausea/vomiting), 3 clinical signs (right lower quadrant pain, rebound tenderness, fever) and 2 laboratory findings (leukocytosis and left shift), with the mnemonic MANTRELS, totaling 10 points.

    History

    Widely used in emergency services as a triage tool for patients with suspected acute appendicitis, aiding in the decision regarding the need for complementary imaging or direct surgical evaluation.

    NANDA — Acute pain

    Unpleasant sensory and emotional experience associated with actual or potential tissue damage, with sudden or slow onset, ranging from mild to intense.

    NANDA — Risk for infection

    Increased susceptibility to invasion by pathogenic organisms, related to possible abdominal inflammatory/infectious process.

    NANDA — Nausea

    Unpleasant subjective sensation, wave-like, in the back of the throat, epigastrium, or abdomen, which may or may not lead to vomiting.

    NOC — Pain control

    • Reports pain controlled or under active investigation
    • Communicates changes in pain pattern to the team

    NIC — Acute abdominal pain management

    • Assess pain characteristics (location, migration, intensity)
    • Apply the Alvarado Score systematically
    • Collect laboratory tests as per medical order
    • Communicate elevated score to medical/surgical team
    • Keep patient NPO until diagnostic definition, per medical order

    Scientific Validation

    Validation studies demonstrate good sensitivity for high scores (7 or more), although specificity varies according to the studied population and disease prevalence.

    Limitations

    It does not replace clinical judgment or imaging in cases of diagnostic doubt, especially in children, elderly, pregnant women, and women of childbearing age, populations in which the score may have reduced performance.

    How the Alvarado Score works

    The Alvarado Score sums points from 8 criteria (mnemonic MANTRELS): migration of pain, anorexia, nausea/vomiting, right lower quadrant pain, rebound tenderness, fever, leukocytosis, and left shift, totaling 0 to 10 points.

    Quick guide: 0-4 = Low probability | 5-6 = Possible appendicitis | 7-8 = Probable appendicitis | 9-10 = Very likely appendicitis.

    Does the Alvarado Score replace computed tomography?

    Not necessarily. In cases of intermediate score (5-6) or diagnostic doubt, especially in special populations, complementary imaging (ultrasound or CT) is often recommended.

    Is the Alvarado Score reliable in children and pregnant women?

    The performance of the score may be reduced in these populations, and it is always recommended to complement with detailed clinical evaluation and imaging when there is any diagnostic doubt.
