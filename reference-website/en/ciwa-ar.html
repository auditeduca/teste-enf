About this tool

To objectively assess the severity of alcohol withdrawal syndrome, guiding the need for pharmacological treatment and the level of monitoring required. Indicated for patients with a history of heavy alcohol use undergoing detoxification, especially in the first 72 hours after the last drink.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–9: Mild Withdrawal
  • 10–18: Moderate Withdrawal
  • 19–67: Severe Withdrawal

Important tips

  • Reassess CIWA-Ar serially (usually every 1-2 hours in the first 24-72 hours) to monitor the evolution of the condition.
  • Symptom-triggered therapy (using CIWA-Ar) tends to use less total medication than fixed-dose regimens.
  • Hallucinations present in any of the sensory items significantly increase risk and warrant immediate attention.
  • Maintain a calm environment with reduced stimuli to help control symptoms.

    Foundation

    The CIWA-Ar (Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol, Revised) was developed by Sullivan and colleagues in 1989, assessing 10 items related to signs and symptoms of alcohol withdrawal, allowing objective quantification of the severity of the syndrome and guiding the need for pharmacological treatment.

    History

    Widely used in hospital alcohol detoxification protocols, allowing symptom-triggered therapy, which reduces the total amount of medication used compared to fixed-dose regimens.

    NANDA — Acute Confusion

    Abrupt onset of reversible disturbances of consciousness, attention, cognition, and perception that develop over a short period.

    NANDA — Anxiety

    Vague and uncomfortable feeling of discomfort or fear, accompanied by autonomic response.

    NANDA — Risk for falls

    Increased vulnerability to falls, associated with tremor, agitation, and altered mental status.

    NOC — Severity of substance withdrawal symptoms

    • Reduction of autonomic signs (tremor, sweating)
    • Absence of hallucinations
    • Preserved orientation

    NIC — Substance withdrawal management: alcohol

    • Apply CIWA-Ar systematically and serially
    • Administer medication according to prescription and obtained score
    • Maintain a safe environment with reduced stimuli
    • Monitor vital signs and level of consciousness
    • Immediately communicate signs of worsening to the medical team

    Scientific Validation

    Extensively validated in the literature, considered the gold standard for assessing the severity of alcohol withdrawal in clinical settings, with good reliability when applied by trained professionals.

    Limitations

    Requires adequate staff training for standardized application. Should not be applied to patients with significant decreased level of consciousness due to other causes, which may compromise the assessment of subjective items.

    How the CIWA-Ar works

    The CIWA-Ar sums points from 10 items related to signs and symptoms of alcohol withdrawal — nausea/vomiting, tremor, sweating, anxiety, agitation, tactile, auditory, and visual disturbances, headache, and orientation — totaling 0 to 67 points.

    Quick guide: 0-9 = Mild | 10-18 = Moderate | ≥19 = Severe, risk of delirium tremens.

    Can CIWA-Ar be used in any patient with decreased level of consciousness?

    No. CIWA-Ar depends on subjective items reported by the patient, being less reliable in patients with significant decreased level of consciousness due to other associated causes.

    How often should CIWA-Ar be reassessed?

    Generally every 1 to 2 hours in the first 24 to 72 hours after the last alcohol intake, or per institutional protocol, especially in patients with high scores.
