About this tool

To identify whether a chronic pain condition has a predominant neuropathic mechanism, combining 5 questionnaire items on symptoms and 2 clinical sensory examination items (allodynia and altered pinprick threshold). Indicated for patients with chronic pain of any origin to differentiate predominantly neuropathic pain from nociceptive pain, aiding in therapeutic choice.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–11: Neuropathic Mechanism Unlikely
  • 12–24: Neuropathic Mechanism Likely

Important tips

  • Unlike many scales, LANSS items have different weights (1, 2, 3, or 5 points), not a uniform score — pay attention to the weight of each positive item.
  • The 2 physical examination items (allodynia and needle-prick threshold) require comparison between the painful area and a non-painful/contralateral area of the patient.
  • A score equal to or greater than 12 suggests a predominant neuropathic mechanism, guiding the choice of specific therapy (e.g., anticonvulsants, antidepressants).
  • The LANSS does not replace clinical judgment — about 10-20% of patients with neuropathic pain may not be identified by the screening tool alone.

    Foundation

    The LANSS combines 5 questionnaire items on sensory symptoms (weighted 1 to 5 points) and 2 clinical sensory examination items — allodynia (5 points) and altered needle-prick threshold (3 points) — totaling a maximum score of 24 points.

    History

    Developed by Michael Bennett in 2001 in Leeds (United Kingdom), it was one of the first validated instruments to clinically differentiate neuropathic pain from nociceptive pain at the bedside, without the need for additional tests.

    NANDA — Chronic pain

    Unpleasant sensory and emotional experience associated with actual or potential tissue damage, or described in such terms, with gradual or sudden onset, mild to intense intensity, constant or recurrent, without anticipated or predictable end.

    NANDA — Disturbed Sensory Perception

    Change in the amount or pattern of stimuli received, accompanied by a diminished, exaggerated, distorted, or impaired response to such stimuli, related to sensory changes identified on examination.

    NOC — Pain control

    • LANSS score assessed to characterize the predominant pain mechanism
    • Therapy adjusted according to identified pain mechanism (neuropathic vs. nociceptive)
    • Reduction in reported pain intensity after targeted intervention

    NIC — Neuropathic Pain Management

    • Apply the LANSS by combining the symptom questionnaire and sensory examination (allodynia and needle-prick threshold)
    • Communicate to the medical team the suspicion of neuropathic mechanism when the score is ≥12
    • Collaborate in the introduction of specific therapy for neuropathic pain as prescribed
    • Educate the patient about the nature of neuropathic pain and treatment expectations
    • Periodically reassess the response to the instituted treatment

    Scientific Validation

    Validated in various chronic pain conditions, including cancer pain and chronic low back pain, with sensitivity between 82-91% and specificity between 80-94% for identifying predominantly neuropathic pain.

    Sensitivity: 85% · Specificity: 87%

    Limitations

    Screening tools like the LANSS may fail to identify 10-20% of patients with neuropathic pain, making it essential to combine clinical judgment and complete physical examination findings with the scale score.

    How the LANSS Scale Works

    The LANSS sums variable weights (1 to 5 points) from 5 symptom items and 2 physical examination items (allodynia and altered pinprick threshold), totaling up to 24 points.

    Quick guide: <12 = Neuropathic mechanism unlikely | ≥12 = Neuropathic mechanism likely.

    Can the LANSS be applied without the physical examination (only the questionnaire)?

    The original LANSS version mandatorily includes the 2 physical examination items (allodynia and needle-prick threshold). There is a self-report version without physical examination, called S-LANSS, but with slightly lower sensitivity than the original version.

    Does a low LANSS score completely exclude the presence of neuropathic pain?

    Not entirely. Screening tools like LANSS may fail to identify 10-20% of patients with neuropathic pain, making it essential to combine the scale result with clinical judgment and a detailed patient history.
