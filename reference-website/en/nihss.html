About this tool

To objectively quantify the severity of neurological deficit caused by a stroke, through the assessment of 11 neurological items (15 scored sub-items). Indicated in the initial and serial evaluation of patients with suspected acute stroke, to quantify severity, guide therapeutic decisions (e.g., thrombolysis), and monitor clinical evolution.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–0: No Stroke Symptoms
  • 1–4: Mild Stroke
  • 5–15: Moderate Stroke
  • 16–20: Moderate to Severe Stroke
  • 21–42: Severe Stroke

Important tips

  • The NIHSS should be administered by a trained and ideally certified examiner, strictly following the standardized instructions for each item.
  • Items 5 (arm) and 6 (leg) are assessed separately for each side, each pair summing up to 8 points (4+4).
  • An NIHSS score of 0 does not rule out stroke — posterior fossa/cerebellar lesions may present with subtle deficits not fully captured by the scale.
  • The NIHSS is essential for therapeutic decisions in the acute phase (e.g., eligibility for thrombolysis/thrombectomy) and should be serially reassessed to detect early neurological worsening.

    Foundation

    The NIHSS assesses 11 neurological items, some divided into sub-items (totaling 15 summed scores): level of consciousness, questions and commands (LOC), conjugate gaze, visual fields, facial palsy, motor strength of the 4 limbs, limb ataxia, sensory, language, dysarthria, and extinction/neglect. The total score ranges from 0 to 42 points.

    History

    Developed by Patrick Lyden and colleagues for the NINDS rt-PA Stroke Trial study, it was published in 1989 (Brott et al.) and became the gold-standard scale for quantifying stroke severity in clinical trials and clinical practice.

    NANDA — Ineffective cerebral tissue perfusion

    Decreased cerebral blood circulation, capable of compromising health, identified by neurological deficits measured on the NIHSS.

    NANDA — Impaired physical mobility

    Impaired independent physical mobility, related to motor deficits identified in items 5 and 6 of the scale.

    NANDA — Impaired Verbal Communication

    Decreased ability to receive, process, transmit, and/or use a system of symbols, related to aphasia and/or dysarthria identified in items 9 and 10.

    NANDA — Risk for aspiration

    Risk of entry of gastrointestinal secretions, oropharyngeal secretions, solids, or liquids into the tracheobronchial passages, related to deficits in level of consciousness and/or dysphagia associated with stroke.

    NOC — Tissue Perfusion: Cerebral

    • NIHSS assessed on admission and reassessed serially
    • Absence of worsening of NIHSS score over time
    • Reperfusion therapy initiated within the therapeutic window when indicated

    NIC — Neurologic Monitoring

    • Apply the NIHSS in a standardized manner on admission and at regular intervals according to institutional protocol
    • Immediately report any increase in score (neurological worsening) to the medical team
    • Check eligibility and therapeutic window for thrombolysis/thrombectomy
    • Monitor vital signs, blood glucose, and oxygen saturation
    • Assess swallowing before initiating oral diet, given the risk of dysphagia
    • Record the NIHSS score and time of assessment in the medical record

    Scientific Validation

    Widely validated internationally, with good inter-rater reliability, especially when raters are certified. It is a strong predictor of functional outcome and brain lesion volume.

    Limitations

    It assigns greater weight to language deficits (left hemisphere) than to equivalent deficits of the right hemisphere, potentially underestimating the severity of non-dominant hemisphere strokes. A score of 0 does not rule out stroke, especially in posterior fossa/cerebellar lesions. Training and certification are recommended for reliable application.

    How the NIHSS (National Institutes of Health Stroke Scale) works

    The NIHSS sums the scores of 15 neurological sub-items (level of consciousness, gaze, visual fields, facial palsy, motor strength of the four limbs, ataxia, sensory, language, dysarthria, and extinction/neglect), ranging from 0 to 42 points.

    Quick guide: 0 = No symptoms | 1-4 = Mild | 5-15 = Moderate | 16-20 = Moderate to severe | 21-42 = Severe.

    Can the NIHSS be used alone to decide on thrombolysis?

    No. Although it is an important component of the assessment, the American Heart Association/American Stroke Association guidelines recommend that the NIHSS should not be used as the sole criterion for eligibility or exclusion from reperfusion therapies, and should be combined with complete clinical and imaging evaluation.

    What is the difference between the NIHSS and the modified NIHSS (mNIHSS)?

    The mNIHSS is a shortened and validated version of the NIHSS that removes items 1a (level of consciousness), 4 (facial palsy), and 7 (limb ataxia), being faster to administer and, according to some studies, equally or more reliable.
