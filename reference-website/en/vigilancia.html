High-Alert Medications (HAMs)

List of main HAMs, their importance, traceability, essential checklists, relationship with double/triple checking and the International Patient Safety Goals.

What are High-Alert Medications (HAMs)?

High-Alert Medications (HAMs) are those that have a higher risk of causing serious or even fatal harm to the patient when administered incorrectly, even when standard procedures are followed. Therefore, these medications require additional care in their storage, prescription, preparation, dispensing, and administration.

The Ministry of Health, international organizations such as the Institute for Safe Medication Practices (ISMP) and the Brazilian Institute for Patient Safety (IBSP), indicate that these medications are not necessarily more dangerous by nature, but rather because any error related to them is more likely to cause significant harm.

Why Vigilance?

Imagine that this medication is so important that it needs to have control within Hospitals and Health Institutions that must be "Monitored" from the moment it leaves the pharmacy dispensary until its final destination, which is administration to the patient... Below are types of questions that guide High-Alert Medications (HAMs):

Where was it taken from?
At what time?
Which presentation was dispensed?
Who has it?
Where is it?
Who is it for?
Who will prepare it?
Where is it stored?
Who requested it?
Who checked the preparation?
Who administered it?

This type of medication requires this overt vigilance as explained above. An error or a "near-miss" effect can trigger what we call: SENTINEL EVENT or ADVERSE EVENT.

What is a Sentinel Event?

Um A sentinel event is a serious and unexpected occurrence, usually in healthcare, that results in death, permanent or severe harm to the patient (or staff/visitors), serving as a warning sign for systemic failures that need to be investigated and corrected, such as surgery on the wrong site or retained material. They are called "sentinel" because they point to larger problems, requiring root cause analysis and continuous improvement.

Key Characteristics:

  • Unexpected and Severe: Not related to the patient's natural disease.
  • Warning Sign: Indicates problems in the system that may lead to further incidents.
  • Mandatory to Report: Requires investigation and in-depth analysis.

And what are the High-Alert Medications (HAMs)?

There are numerous medications that qualify as High-Alert Medications (HAMs). Which ones they are depends on WHICH healthcare institution we are talking about. For example: General Hospitals usually do not have many Neoplastics, whereas oncology hospitals do. Polyclinics usually do not have many medications called Vasoactive Drugs, whereas general hospitals do. So this will depend on the type of healthcare facility the professional is in. In some hospitals, Antimicrobials, Neuromuscular Blockers, Blood Products, and Enteral Nutrition are included in the HAM list. Flow from Dispensation to Administration

1. INITIAL CHECK

Check the medication released by the pharmacy against what was prescribed together with the clinical pharmacist.

2. SAFE STORAGE

Upon receiving the medication from the pharmacy to the ward/ICU/Surgical block, check WHERE the drug will be stored. Some drugs require adequate temperature, needing refrigeration like Insulins, others protection from light. Most importantly, High-Alert Medications (HAMs) are usually only released when it is very close to the time of administration, so be careful where you leave them. Preferably, only handle them when preparing.

3. DOUBLE CHECKING (Mandatory)

It must be performed with your nurse. Do not perform double checking with another nursing technician. Remember: this medication is a HAM! Check the 10 Rights of Medication Administration.

4. TRIPLE CHECKING

Certain medications require extra safety. Who participates? Besides your nurse, the prescribing physician or clinical pharmacist. See more at Double and Triple Checking.

5. PREPARE THE MEDICATION

Ideally, it should be prepared under the supervision of your nurse, during double checking, and sometimes certain HAM drugs should be prepared by the nurse themselves, such as neoplastics, blood products, and parenteral nutrition.

6. ADMINISTRATION

Follow the 10 steps again at the patient's bedside; International Goal 1: Correct patient identification and International Goal 5: Hand hygiene. If there are doubts from the patient or companion, do your best to clarify them. If you don't know or if there is insecurity on the patient's part, call your nurse to explain.

Summary

High Alert for medications is, as the name says, constant vigilance, from removal of the medication from the pharmacy to administration due to the high probability of risks of adverse or sentinel events. Do not be afraid to prepare these medications but always apply checklists, follow the protocols established by the hospital, and ALWAYS apply double and triple checking. The prescription may be obviously correct and administration routine, but double and triple checking alerts other professionals: "Oops! This medication is circulating." The "internal watch button" of your nurse and other professionals will be on. NEVER under any circumstances prepare a high-alert medication alone (alone meaning only you know you are preparing it). We make mistakes, sometimes we are tired, or sometimes we simply need an EXTRA alert about that drug. VIGILANCE!
