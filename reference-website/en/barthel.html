About this tool

To assess in detail the patient's degree of functional independence in 10 activities of daily living, with scores proportional to the level of assistance needed. Indicated in rehabilitation, geriatrics, pre- and post-discharge evaluation, and longitudinal follow-up of functional capacity.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–20: Total Dependence
  • 21–60: Severe Dependence
  • 61–90: Moderate Dependence
  • 91–99: Mild Dependence
  • 100–100: Total Independence

Important tips

  • Assess actual observed performance, not the theoretical ability reported by the patient.
  • Combine with the Lawton Scale for a complete view of functionality (ADLs + IADLs).
  • Use the same scale in serial assessments to track functional evolution.
  • Consider the use of assistive devices when scoring each item.

    Foundation

    The Barthel Index was developed by Florence Mahoney and Dorothea Barthel in 1965, offering a more granular assessment of functional independence than simple binary scales, by assigning different weights according to task complexity.

    History

    Widely used in rehabilitation, geriatrics, and international clinical research, being one of the most cited functional assessment instruments in the literature.

    NANDA — Self-care deficit: bathing

    Impaired ability to perform or complete bathing/hygiene activities by oneself.

    NANDA — Impaired physical mobility

    Limitation in independent voluntary movement of body parts or the whole body.

    NANDA — Bowel/urinary incontinence

    Change in normal bowel or urinary elimination pattern, characterized by involuntary elimination.

    NOC — Self-care: activities of daily living

    • Performs personal hygiene
    • Dresses without help
    • Feeds without help
    • Mobilizes safely

    NIC — Self-care assistance

    • Assess detailed functional capacity
    • Encourage residual independence
    • Adapt environment and assistive devices
    • Plan discharge with adequate support

    Scientific Validation

    Extensively validated in diverse populations, with good interobserver reliability and sensitivity to detect changes over time, useful for monitoring progress in rehabilitation processes.

    Limitations

    Does not assess more complex instrumental activities (finances, telephone, shopping) — for that, it is recommended to associate the Lawton Scale. May have a ceiling effect in highly functional patients.

    How the Barthel Index works

    The Barthel Index assesses 10 activities of daily living — feeding, bathing, grooming, dressing, bowel and bladder control, toilet use, transfer, mobility, and stair climbing — with scores proportional to the degree of independence in each. The total ranges from 0 to 100 points.

    Quick guide: 100 = Total independence | 91-99 = Mild dependence | 61-90 = Moderate | 21-60 = Severe | 0-20 = Total.

    How often should I reapply the Barthel Index?

    Reassessment is recommended at admission, discharge, and periodically during rehabilitation processes to document functional evolution.

    Is the Barthel more detailed than the Katz?

    Yes. The Barthel uses a weighted point scale (0, 5, 10, or 15 per item) that reflects the degree of assistance needed, while the Katz uses only binary scoring (independent/dependent).
