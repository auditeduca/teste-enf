About this tool

Identify hospitalized pediatric patients at increased risk of falls, based on 7 clinical and developmental factors. Indicated for fall risk screening in hospitalized pediatric patients at admission and reassessed periodically during hospitalization.

Interpretation ranges

  • 7–11: Low Fall Risk
  • 12–23: High Fall Risk

Important tips

  • The Humpty Dumpty Scale is specific to pediatric patients — it should not be applied to adults.
  • The factor 'history of previous falls' in the environmental factors domain represents the greatest isolated warning sign in this domain.
  • Children under 3 years receive the highest score on the age factor, due to the physical instability and lack of risk perception inherent to this age group.
  • Reassess the score whenever there is a change in the child's clinical status, cognitive status, or medication.

    Foundation

    The Humpty Dumpty Scale assesses 7 parameters — age, sex, diagnosis, cognitive impairment, environmental factors, response to surgery/sedation/anesthesia, and medication use — each scored according to a specific table, summing to a total score between 7 and 23 points.

    History

    Developed specifically for the hospitalized pediatric population, unlike fall risk scales for adults, it incorporates developmental factors inherent to childhood, such as the physical instability characteristic of certain age groups.

    NANDA — Risk for falls

    Increased susceptibility to falls, which may cause physical harm and compromise health, related to the child's developmental and clinical factors.

    NANDA — Risk of physical trauma

    Increased susceptibility to accidental tissue injury, related to age, mobility, and hospital environment.

    NANDA — Dysfunctional family processes

    Family functioning that cannot support the well-being of its members, relevant when intensive caregiver education about child supervision is needed.

    NOC — Fall Occurrence

    • Humpty Dumpty score assessed at admission and reassessed periodically
    • Fall prevention protocol implemented according to identified risk
    • Family educated about safety measures and supervision

    NIC — Fall prevention in pediatrics

    • Apply the Humpty Dumpty Scale at admission and reassess periodically
    • Implement visual risk identification (bracelet, door/bed signage) per institutional protocol
    • Educate parents/caregivers about the importance of not leaving the high-risk child unsupervised
    • Keep bed rails raised and environment free of obstacles
    • Review sedative or risk medications with medical/pharmacy team

    Scientific Validation

    Validated in case-control studies in the pediatric hospital setting, demonstrating good sensitivity in identifying high-risk patients, and is widely adopted in pediatric patient safety protocols.

    Limitations

    Should not be used in adult patients, as it was developed and validated specifically for the pediatric population. Revised versions of the scale (HDFS 2.0) removed the sex and medication parameters and adjusted the age score, which may yield differences compared to the original version used here.

    How the Humpty Dumpty Scale works

    The Humpty Dumpty Scale sums the scores of 7 parameters — age, sex, diagnosis, cognitive impairment, environmental factors, response to surgery/sedation/anesthesia, and medication use — totaling 7 to 23 points.

    Quick guide: 7-11 points = Low risk | ≥12 points = High fall risk.

    Is the Humpty Dumpty Scale for adults?

    No. It is validated specifically for the hospitalized pediatric population. For adults, other specific tools are used, such as the Morse Scale or the Hendrich II Fall Risk Model.

    Is there an updated version of the scale?

    Yes. The revised version (HDFS 2.0) removed the sex and medication parameters and adjusted the age factor score, aiming to improve the predictive performance of the tool. This calculator uses the original 7-item version, widely adopted in institutional protocols.

    Important Notice

    This tool is for educational and clinical decision support purposes. Results should always be reviewed and validated by a qualified nursing professional, according to patient assessment and institutional protocols.
