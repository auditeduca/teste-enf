About this tool

Identify hospitalized adult patients at increased risk of falls based on clinical factors and performance on the Get-Up-and-Go test. Indicated for fall risk screening in hospitalized adult patients, usually on admission and reassessed periodically.

Interpretation ranges

  • 0–4: Low Fall Risk
  • 5–16: High Fall Risk

Important tips

  • The Get-Up-and-Go test can contribute up to 4 of the total 16 points — observe the patient's performance carefully when rising from the chair.
  • The cutoff point of 5 points or more for high risk is the most widely used in clinical practice.
  • Reassess the score whenever there is a change in the patient's clinical status, cognition, or medication.
  • Combine the result with observation of environmental factors (lighting, footwear, assistive devices) for a more comprehensive prevention plan.

    Foundation

    The Hendrich II Fall Risk Model assesses 7 clinical risk factors (confusion/disorientation/impulsivity, symptomatic depression, altered elimination, dizziness, male gender, use of antiepileptics, and use of benzodiazepines) added to performance on the Get-Up-and-Go test, totaling a score from 0 to 16 points.

    History

    Developed by Ann Hendrich and collaborators, it is a widely used tool in hospitals for fall risk screening in adult patients, with good predictive validity validated in various hospital settings.

    NANDA — Risk for falls

    Increased susceptibility to falls, which may cause physical harm and compromise health.

    NANDA — Acute Confusion

    Abrupt onset of reversible disturbances of consciousness, attention, cognition, and perception, when present as an identified risk factor.

    NANDA — Impaired physical mobility

    Limitation in independent, intentional physical movement of the body or one or more extremities, related to performance on the Get-Up-and-Go test.

    NOC — Fall Occurrence

    • Hendrich II score assessed on admission and reassessed periodically
    • Fall prevention protocol implemented according to identified risk
    • Absence of falls during hospitalization

    NIC — Fall prevention

    • Apply the Hendrich II Fall Risk Model on admission and reassess periodically
    • Implement visual fall risk identification (wristband, signage) per institutional protocol
    • Review fall risk-associated medications with medical/pharmacy team
    • Educate patient and companions about safety measures
    • Keep bed in low position and call bell within reach

    Scientific Validation

    Validated in multiple studies with good sensitivity and specificity for predicting falls in hospitalized patients, being one of the recommended tools for systematic fall risk screening.

    Limitations

    Like any screening tool, it does not replace individualized clinical judgment. The score should be reassessed whenever there is a change in the patient's clinical status, cognition, or medication.

    How the Hendrich II Fall Risk Model works

    The Hendrich II sums the scores of 7 risk factors (confusion, depression, altered elimination, dizziness, male sex, antiepileptics, and benzodiazepines) with the performance on the Get-Up-and-Go test, totaling 0 to 16 points.

    Quick guide: <5 points = Low risk | ≥5 points = High fall risk.

    Is the Hendrich II used for patients of any age?

    It is primarily validated for hospitalized adult patients. For the pediatric population, specific scales such as the Humpty Dumpty Falls Scale are used.

    How often should the Hendrich II be reassessed?

    It is usually applied on admission and reassessed periodically according to institutional protocol, as well as whenever there is a significant change in the patient's clinical status, cognition, or medication.
